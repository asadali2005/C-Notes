Foundation

Biological Functions of Immunoglobulins

Where Are Immunoglobulins Found?

Definition — Immunoglobulin (Ig)

Immunoglobulins are glycoprotein molecules produced by B cells and plasma cells that serve as antibodies. They specifically recognize and bind antigens — the substances that triggered their production — and subsequently mediate their elimination through various effector mechanisms.

Immunoglobulins are found in two physical forms within the body:

🔗 Membrane-Bound Form
  • Present as transmembrane proteins on the surface of B lymphocytes
  • Function as the B cell receptor (BCR)
  • Recognizes antigen in situ on the cell surface
  • Initiates intracellular signaling upon antigen binding to activate the B cell
  • The BCR is essentially membrane-anchored Ig non-covalently associated with signal-transduction proteins (Igα and Igβ)
💧 Secreted Form (Free Antibody)
  • Secreted by plasma cells — the terminally differentiated end product of B cell activation
  • Circulates freely in blood, lymph, and body secretions
  • Retains same antigen specificity as the BCR of the parent B cell
  • Can reach distant sites to neutralize threats throughout the body
  • The secreted form lacks the transmembrane domain present in the membrane form
Key Point — Plasma Cell

A plasma cell is the "antibody factory" — it is the terminally differentiated B cell whose sole function is mass production and secretion of antibodies. It can secrete thousands of antibody molecules per second. Plasma cells do not express surface BCR and are not capable of further dividing, but are highly specialized secretory machines.

The Two Core Functions of an Immunoglobulin

Every immunoglobulin molecule is a bifunctional entity — one end recognizes the enemy, and the other end calls in the troops to destroy it.

How Papain Cleavage Reveals Fab & Fc

The terms "Fab" and "Fc" come from experiments where the enzyme papain was used to cleave antibodies. Papain cuts at the hinge region (above the disulfide bonds linking the two heavy chains), yielding:

  • Two identical Fab fragments (Fragment, antigen-binding) — each containing one complete light chain and the VH + CH1 domain of the heavy chain
  • One Fc fragment (Fragment, crystallizable) — containing the CH2 and CH3 domains of both heavy chains; named because it crystallized readily in early experiments

Another enzyme, pepsin, cuts below the hinge region, yielding a F(ab')₂ fragment (bivalent, still cross-links antigens) and small pFc' fragments.

Exquisite Antigen Specificity — The Influenza Example

One of the most remarkable properties of the immune system is its ability to generate antibodies of exquisite specificity — each antibody recognizes only one particular epitope. This is illustrated perfectly by the immune response to influenza virus:

Classic Example — Influenza Virus

The influenza virus surface presents two major antigens:

  • Neuraminidase (N) — an enzyme that helps the virus release itself from infected cells and facilitates spread
  • Hemagglutinin (H) — a surface protein that binds sialic acid residues on respiratory epithelial cells, enabling viral attachment and entry

The immune system produces two separate sets of antibodies, each with completely different amino acid sequences in their antigen-binding sites:

  • Anti-N antibodies → recognize neuraminidase specifically
  • Anti-H antibodies → recognize hemagglutinin specifically

By neutralizing both N and H, the antibodies prevent the virus from adhering to respiratory epithelial cells, thereby destroying the infectivity of the virus. This is the principle behind flu vaccination — vaccines present H and N antigens to prime the immune system.

💡
Why specificity matters: The fact that anti-N and anti-H antibodies have different amino acid sequences despite being produced by the same immune system demonstrates somatic hypermutation and the vast diversity of the antibody repertoire (estimated at >1011 distinct specificities).

B Cell Receptor (BCR) vs Secreted Antibody

Antigen recognition by the B cell occurs at two levels:

  • On the cell surface via the BCR: When a naive B cell first encounters its cognate antigen, the BCR (membrane-bound Ig) binds it. This triggers B cell activation, proliferation, and differentiation into plasma cells and memory B cells.
  • By the secreted antibody product: Once activated, the differentiated plasma cells secrete antibodies with the same specificity as the BCR. These circulate throughout the body and can bind the same antigen at distant sites.
Technical Note — BCR Complex

The BCR is not just the membrane Ig alone. It forms a complex with a heterodimer of Igα (CD79a) and Igβ (CD79b) signaling proteins. When antigen binds the membrane Ig, the ITAMs (immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs) on Igα/Igβ are phosphorylated, initiating intracellular signaling cascades that activate the B cell. The membrane Ig recognizes antigen; Igα/β transmit the signal.

Immunoglobulin Class Switching (Isotype Switching)

During the course of an immune response, B cells do not always produce the same class of antibody. The class changes over time — a process critically important for matching the effector function to the threat.

Definition — Class/Isotype Switching

Isotype switching (also called class switching) is the process by which an activated B cell changes the isotype (class) of antibody it produces — from IgM to IgG, IgA, or IgE — without changing the antigen specificity. The variable regions (which determine antigen specificity) are preserved; only the constant region of the heavy chain changes.

Genetic Mechanism — V(D)J Recombination vs Class Switch Recombination

It is important to distinguish two different genetic events:

  • V(D)J recombination (also called somatic recombination): Occurs in immature B cells; generates the diversity of antigen-binding sites by randomly joining V (variable), D (diversity), and J (joining) gene segments. This creates the unique antigen specificity of each B cell clone. This happens before antigen encounter.
  • Class Switch Recombination (CSR): Occurs in activated, antigen-stimulated B cells; changes the constant region of the heavy chain (e.g., from Cμ to Cγ), thereby changing the Ig class from IgM to IgG. The VDJ region is preserved. CSR requires cytokine signals and interaction with CD4+ T helper cells (via CD40–CD40L interaction). This involves a "cascading set of hypermutational events."
Common Confusion to Avoid

Class switching does NOT change antigen specificity. The same antigen is still recognized after switching from IgM to IgG. What changes is the effector function — IgG, for example, can cross the placenta and activate complement more efficiently than IgM can. Antigen specificity is entirely determined by the variable regions (VDJ), which remain unchanged after class switching.

Section Summary — Biological Functions

Core Concepts

  • Ig exists as membrane-bound (BCR) on B cells and secreted antibody from plasma cells
  • Two functions: Antigen recognition (Fab) and Effector function (Fc)
  • Specificity is exquisite — separate antibodies for neuraminidase (N) and hemagglutinin (H) of influenza
  • Class switching: IgM produced early → IgG produced later (same specificity, different effector function)
  • Genetic basis: V(D)J recombination creates diversity; class switch recombination changes the isotype
  • Location matches function: IgM (vascular), IgG (vascular + tissue), sIgA (mucosal surfaces)

Architecture

Immunoglobulin Structure

Basic Architecture of a Monomer

All immunoglobulins share the same fundamental Y-shaped structure, called the basic unit or monomer. Understanding this structure is essential for understanding how all five isotypes work.

Component Number in Monomer Molecular Weight Function
Heavy (H) chains 2 (identical) ~50–77 kDa each Forms the backbone; Fc region; determines isotype
Light (L) chains 2 (identical) ~25 kDa each Part of antigen-binding site; either κ (kappa) or λ (lambda)
Disulfide bonds (inter-chain) Multiple — Link H–H (hinge) and H–L chains together
Intra-chain disulfide bonds ~4–12 per chain — Create compact immunoglobulin-fold "domain" loops

Immunoglobulin Domains

Each heavy and light chain is folded into compact, disulfide-stabilized loops called immunoglobulin (Ig) domains — each ~110 amino acids long. These domains are the fundamental structural unit of the Ig superfamily (which includes MHC, TCR, CD4, CD8, etc.).

Domain Organization by Chain
  • Light chain: 1 variable domain (VL) + 1 constant domain (CL)
  • IgG heavy chain: 1 variable domain (VH) + 3 constant domains (CH1, CH2, CH3)
  • IgM & IgE heavy chain: VH + 4 constant domains (CH1, CH2, CH3, CH4) — note: no hinge region
  • Antigen-binding site: Formed by VH + VL together (hypervariable loops / CDRs within these domains)
  • Fc region: CH2 + CH3 (and CH4 in IgM/IgE)

Variable vs Constant Regions

🎯 Variable (V) Regions
  • Found at the N-terminal end of both H and L chains
  • Amino acid sequence varies enormously between different antibody clones → "variable"
  • Contains Complementarity-Determining Regions (CDRs) — also called hypervariable regions — that directly contact antigen
  • Three CDRs per V domain (CDR1, CDR2, CDR3); CDR3 is most variable
  • Created by V(D)J recombination
  • Determines antigen specificity
⚙️ Constant (C) Regions
  • Found at the C-terminal end of both H and L chains
  • Amino acid sequence is relatively constant within a given isotype
  • The heavy chain C region determines the isotype (class) of the Ig
  • Each isotype has its own type of heavy chain: μ (IgM), γ (IgG), α (IgA), δ (IgD), ε (IgE)
  • Mediates effector functions: complement binding, Fc receptor binding, placental transfer
The Hinge Region

The hinge region is a flexible segment rich in proline and cysteine residues located between the CH1 and CH2 domains of IgG, IgA, and IgD (IgM and IgE lack a hinge region). It provides segmental flexibility, allowing the two Fab arms to rotate and adopt various angles — this is critical for simultaneously cross-linking two identical epitopes on the surface of a pathogen (bivalency). The inter-chain disulfide bonds that link the two heavy chains are in this hinge region.

Section Summary — Structure
  • Monomer: 2 heavy chains + 2 light chains, linked by disulfide bonds → Y-shape
  • Fab = variable regions (VH + VL) + CH1 + CL → antigen binding
  • Fc = CH2 + CH3 (± CH4) → effector functions
  • Variable regions have CDRs that physically contact antigen; created by V(D)J recombination
  • Heavy chain type determines isotype: μ=IgM, γ=IgG, α=IgA, δ=IgD, ε=IgE

Detailed — Isotype 1

IgM — The First Responder

Structure and Physical Characteristics

Definition

IgM is the largest of the immunoglobulin molecules, existing in serum as a pentamer — five Ig monomers joined together by a J (joining) chain and held together by disulfide bonds between the CH4 domains of adjacent μ heavy chains.

Structural Feature Detail Functional Consequence
Pentameric form 5 Ig monomers joined by J-chain and disulfide bonds 10 antigen-binding sites → very efficient agglutinator
Molecular weight ~900 kDa (very large macromolecule) Restricted almost entirely to intravascular space (cannot extravasate)
Heavy chain type μ (mu) — 5 constant domains (no hinge) Activates complement via classical pathway with high efficiency
J chain Polypeptide linking the five monomers at CH4 Required for polymerization; also present in secretory IgA dimer

Functions of IgM

  • Agglutination: The 10 antigen-binding sites make IgM a highly efficient agglutinator of particulate antigens — it can cross-link multiple bacterial or red blood cell surfaces simultaneously, forming large insoluble aggregates that are easily phagocytosed. This is why IgM antibodies are critical in blood typing (ABO system) — anti-A and anti-B are IgM.
  • Complement Activation (Classical Pathway): IgM activates complement with extremely high efficiency. A single IgM molecule (pentamer) binding to antigen is sufficient to bind and activate C1q (the first complement component), triggering the full classical pathway cascade → lysis, opsonization, chemotaxis.
  • First Line of Defense in Primary Response: IgM is synthesized and appears in circulation within days of initial antigen encounter — before IgG is produced. It provides critical early protection against pathogens.
  • Expressed on Naive B Cells (as BCR): IgM (along with IgD) is co-expressed on the surface of naive, antigen-inexperienced B cells as part of the BCR complex. This IgM is a membrane-bound monomer (not a pentamer).
🩸
Clinical relevance — ABO blood typing: The naturally occurring anti-A and anti-B blood group antibodies are of the IgM class. This is why incompatible blood transfusions cause immediate, severe hemolytic reactions — IgM efficiently activates complement and agglutinates foreign red blood cells.

IgM in the Primary Immune Response — Kinetics

The production kinetics of IgM are critical for understanding the body's initial defense:

IgM Kinetics in Primary vs Secondary Response
  • Primary response: IgM appears first (days 3–7 after antigen exposure), peaks rapidly, then declines more rapidly than IgG. This rapid peak and decline is characteristic of IgM.
  • Secondary response: On re-exposure to antigen, IgM is again produced briefly, but the dominant response is a rapid, high-titer IgG response (due to immunological memory and pre-existing class-switched memory B cells).
  • Diagnostic use: Detection of IgM antibodies against a specific pathogen indicates recent, acute infection, whereas IgG indicates past exposure or vaccination.

The Class Switch Mechanism from IgM to IgG

The transition from IgM to IgG (and other isotypes) involves a sophisticated genetic process:

  1. Naive B cell activated: When a B cell encounters its cognate antigen and receives T cell help (CD40–CD40L interaction + cytokines), it is activated and begins proliferating in germinal centers.
  2. Cytokine signals specify the switch: Different cytokines specify different isotype switches. For example: IL-4 → IgE; TGF-β → IgA; IFN-γ → IgG2a (in mice). These signals activate the transcription of specific switch (S) regions upstream of each constant region gene.
  3. AID (Activation-Induced Cytidine Deaminase) acts: AID introduces mutations in the switch regions, initiating DNA double-strand breaks.
  4. DNA between S regions is deleted: The DNA between the Sμ (IgM) switch region and (e.g.) Sγ (IgG) switch region is looped out and deleted. The VHDJH exon (which encodes antigen specificity) is now juxtaposed with the Cγ exon instead of Cμ.
  5. New mRNA and protein produced: The B cell now transcribes and translates IgG instead of IgM — with the same antigen-binding specificity but new effector function. This class switch is irreversible and heritable.
Important — What Cascading Hypermutational Events Means

The notes state that the class switch involves "a cascading set of hypermutational events." This refers to the coordinated action of AID and error-prone DNA repair mechanisms. Importantly, somatic hypermutation (SHM) also occurs in germinal centers (in the V regions, increasing affinity), but SHM and class switch recombination (CSR) are distinct processes — both mediated by AID, but targeting different DNA regions. V(D)J recombination (generating initial diversity) is yet another, earlier process mediated by RAG1/RAG2 enzymes.

Clinical Connection — Hyper-IgM Syndrome (HIGM)

Clinical Condition — Hyper-IgM Syndrome (HIGM)

Hyper-IgM Syndrome (HIGM) is a primary immunodeficiency disorder characterized by:

  • Markedly elevated serum IgM levels (hyperimmunoglobulinemia M)
  • Markedly decreased or absent IgG, IgA, and IgE
  • Resultant susceptibility to recurrent bacterial infections (because protective IgG and mucosal IgA are absent)

Molecular cause: The most common form (HIGM type 1, X-linked) is caused by mutations in the gene encoding CD40 ligand (CD40L / CD154) on T cells. Less commonly, mutations in CD40 on B cells cause HIGM type 3.

Why this causes HIGM: The CD40–CD40L interaction between T helper cells and B cells provides the critical contact signal required for class switch recombination. Without this signal:

  • B cells can still produce IgM (which does not require class switching)
  • B cells cannot switch to IgG, IgA, or IgE → IgM accumulates
  • The hyperproduction of IgM occurs because there is no class switching as an "exit pathway"

Clinical implications: Patients suffer from recurrent bacterial infections (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae), Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia, and Cryptosporidium infections (the last two because IgA and cell-mediated immunity via CD40 are also impaired). Treated with IV immunoglobulin (IVIG) replacement and prophylactic antibiotics.

Section Summary — IgM
  • Largest Ig: Pentamer, ~900 kDa, 10 antigen-binding sites
  • Location: Restricted to intravascular space due to large size
  • Role: First antibody of primary response; excellent agglutinator and complement activator
  • Kinetics: Peaks early, declines rapidly; IgM serology → acute infection marker
  • Class switch: IgM → IgG/IgA/IgE requires CD40–CD40L interaction + AID-mediated recombination
  • HIGM: No CD40L/CD40 → no class switching → excess IgM, absent IgG/IgA → recurrent infections

Detailed — Isotype 2

IgG — The Workhorse Antibody

Key Properties of IgG

Overview

IgG is the most abundant immunoglobulin in human serum, comprising approximately 75–80% of total serum immunoglobulin. It is a monomer (~150 kDa) with 2 antigen-binding sites, and represents the dominant antibody of the secondary (memory) immune response.

Property Detail Clinical/Functional Significance
Most abundant Ig ~8–16 mg/mL serum; 75–80% of total Ig Major effector antibody in the blood and tissues
Distribution Both intravascular AND extravascular spaces Readily diffuses between blood and interstitial tissues; broad reach
Half-life ~23 days (longest of all Ig) Provides long-lasting protection; maintenance of humoral immunity; basis of passive immunization durability
Placental transfer Yes — the only Ig that crosses the placenta Provides passive neonatal immunity for the first 3–6 months of life until the infant's own immune system matures. Transferred via FcRn (neonatal Fc receptor) on placental syncytiotrophoblasts.
Complement activation Via classical pathway (IgG1 and IgG3 most efficient) Opsonization, lysis of bacteria/complement-sensitive cells
Protective against Bacteria, viruses, parasites, some fungi Broad protective antibody
👶
Why newborns aren't immunologically naked: Maternal IgG is actively transported across the placenta from ~22 weeks gestation onward via FcRn. At birth, infant serum IgG ≈ maternal IgG. This provides protection against pathogens the mother has been exposed to or vaccinated against. Serum IgG in the infant reaches a nadir at ~3–6 months as maternal IgG is catabolized and infant IgG synthesis is still ramping up — this is the period of "physiologic hypogammaglobulinemia of infancy."

IgG Subclasses

In humans, IgG has four subclasses — IgG1, IgG2, IgG3, IgG4 — reflecting different γ heavy chains (γ1, γ2, γ3, γ4). They differ in their hinge region length, number of inter-chain disulfide bonds, half-life, complement activation efficiency, and Fc receptor binding affinity.

Subclass Serum % of IgG Half-life Complement Main Features
IgG1 ~65% 23 days ++ Good Responds to protein antigens; most efficient at ADCC; highest FcγR binding
IgG2 ~25% 23 days + Moderate Responds primarily to polysaccharide antigens (e.g., bacterial capsules); poor FcγR binding; low ADCC
IgG3 ~7% 7 days (shortest — due to unique hinge) +++ Best Best complement activator; very long hinge (flexible); important in viral infections
IgG4 ~3% 23 days – Poor Induced by chronic antigen exposure; anti-inflammatory; Fab arm exchange (functionally monovalent); IgG4-related disease (IgG4-RD)

Fc Receptors (FcγR) — Connecting Antibody to Effector Cells

The Fc region of IgG interacts with Fc gamma receptors (FcγR) expressed on various immune effector cells. This interaction bridges humoral immunity (antibodies) and cellular immunity (phagocytes, NK cells).

Cells Expressing FcγR (Fc Receptors for IgG)

The notes specify the following cell types express Fc receptors for IgG:

  • Monocytes — circulating precursors of macrophages; express FcγRI (high affinity), FcγRII, FcγRIII
  • Macrophages — tissue-resident; major phagocytes; engulf IgG-opsonized pathogens
  • Dendritic cells — antigen-presenting cells; FcγR helps capture opsonized antigens for presentation to T cells
  • Neutrophils — express FcγRIII and FcγRII; rapid phagocytosis and oxidative burst against IgG-coated bacteria
  • NK cells — express FcγRIII (CD16); critical for ADCC (see next section)
🔑
Opsonization: When IgG antibodies bind to the surface of a bacterium (coating it), they serve as opsonins — tags that phagocytes recognize via their FcγR. This dramatically enhances the efficiency of phagocytosis. This is one of the most important protective mechanisms of IgG antibodies.

ADCC — Antibody-Dependent Cell-Mediated Cytotoxicity

Definition — ADCC

Antibody-Dependent Cell-Mediated Cytotoxicity (ADCC) is a mechanism by which immune effector cells (especially NK cells) kill antibody-coated target cells. It forms a bridge between humoral and cellular immunity — the antibody provides the specificity, the NK cell provides the killing power.

The notes state: "Target cells coated with IgG antibodies directed against cell surface antigens may be eliminated through this ADCC mechanism." Here is the complete step-by-step mechanism:

  1. Target cell expression of surface antigen: The target cell (e.g., a virus-infected cell, tumor cell, or foreign cell) expresses foreign or abnormal antigens on its surface. These antigens were recognized during the immune response, and IgG antibodies were made against them.
  2. IgG opsonization of target cell: Specific IgG antibodies bind their cognate antigen on the target cell surface via the Fab regions. The Fc regions of the bound IgG antibodies now point outward from the target cell surface, accessible to Fc receptors on nearby NK cells.
  3. NK cell recognition via FcγRIII (CD16): A cytotoxic NK cell approaches the IgG-coated target cell. The NK cell's FcγRIII (CD16) receptor binds the Fc portion of the surface-bound IgG antibodies. This interaction brings the NK cell into close proximity to the target cell.
  4. NK cell activation: The binding of FcγRIII to the IgG-Fc region triggers signaling cascades within the NK cell, activating it to release cytotoxic granules.
  5. Apoptosis of target cell: The activated NK cell releases its cytotoxic granules at the immunological synapse (the contact zone between NK cell and target). Granules contain:
    • Perforin: polymerizes in the target cell membrane to form pores
    • Granzymes (A, B, etc.): serine proteases that enter through perforin pores and activate caspase-dependent and -independent apoptotic pathways within the target cell
  6. Target cell dies by apoptosis: The target cell undergoes programmed cell death (apoptosis). The NK cell is spared and can move on to kill additional antibody-coated targets.
Why ADCC Is Immunologically Elegant

ADCC solves a fundamental problem: how does the immune system kill an infected cell or tumor cell that has foreign proteins but cannot be easily phagocytosed (e.g., large cells)? ADCC provides the answer — the antibody acts as a molecular bridge, connecting the specificity of humoral immunity (the antigen-binding sites identifying the target) to the killing power of cellular immunity (NK cells). The antibody "labels" the target; the NK cell does the killing via apoptosis.

Clinical Relevance — ADCC in Therapeutics

ADCC is the mechanism of action of many therapeutic monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) used in cancer treatment:

  • Trastuzumab (Herceptin®) — anti-HER2 mAb for breast cancer; ADCC kills HER2-overexpressing tumor cells
  • Rituximab (Rituxan®) — anti-CD20 mAb for B cell lymphomas; ADCC kills CD20+ tumor B cells
  • Cetuximab — anti-EGFR mAb for colorectal/head-neck cancers
    • These drugs are engineered or selected for optimal Fc region binding to FcγRIII on NK cells to maximize ADCC.
Section Summary — IgG
  • Most abundant: 75-80% of serum Ig, ~8–16 mg/mL
  • Location: Both intravascular and extravascular spaces (small size allows diffusion)
  • Longest half-life: ~23 days (FcRn-mediated recycling)
  • Only Ig crossing the placenta → passive neonatal immunity
  • Activates complement: Via classical pathway (IgG1 > IgG3 > IgG2)
  • FcγR: Expressed on monocytes, macrophages, DCs, neutrophils, NK cells → opsonization + ADCC
  • ADCC: IgG coats target → NK cell CD16 binds Fc → perforin/granzyme release → target apoptosis
  • 4 subclasses: IgG1 (most), IgG3 (best complement), IgG2 (polysaccharides), IgG4 (anti-inflammatory)

Additional Isotypes

IgA, IgD, and IgE — The Other Isotypes

IgA — The Mucosal Guardian

IgA Key Facts
  • Structure: Monomer in serum; dimer in secretions (sIgA), connected by J chain and a secretory component (SC) — the SC protects sIgA from proteolytic degradation in the harsh mucosal environment
  • Location: Mucosal surfaces — respiratory tract, GI tract, urogenital tract, breast milk, saliva, tears. The most abundant Ig at mucosal surfaces; second most abundant in serum.
  • Function: "First line of defense" against pathogens entering via mucosal routes (influenza, HIV). sIgA neutralizes pathogens before they can cross the mucosal epithelium — immune exclusion.
  • Transport mechanism: Local submucosal plasma cells secrete dimeric IgA → binds polymeric Ig receptor (pIgR) on the basolateral surface of epithelial cells → transcytosed to the apical surface → SC cleaved off the pIgR but remains associated → sIgA released into lumen
  • Complement: sIgA does NOT activate the classical pathway (cannot bind C1q); activates alternative and lectin pathways only. This is important — complement activation in mucosae would cause harmful inflammation.
  • Subclasses: IgA1 (predominates in serum and upper respiratory tract) and IgA2 (predominates in lower GI tract; more resistant to bacterial proteases)

IgD — The Enigmatic Isotype

IgD Key Facts
  • Structure: Monomer; unique δ heavy chain; very long hinge region
  • Location: Predominantly on the surface of naive, mature B cells (co-expressed with IgM as part of the BCR complex)
  • Serum levels: Very low (<1% of total serum Ig); very short half-life (~3 days)
  • Function: Role in signaling B cell maturation and in setting the threshold for B cell activation; its exact role is not fully established. Mice lacking IgD are relatively immunologically normal, suggesting redundancy with IgM on the BCR.
  • Secreted IgD: A secreted form of IgD has recently been found at mucosal surfaces (upper respiratory mucosa), where it may activate basophils and innate immune responses — an emerging area of research.

IgE — The Allergy Antibody

IgE Key Facts
  • Structure: Monomer; ε heavy chain with 4 C domains (no hinge region); extra CH4 domain
  • Serum levels: The lowest of all isotypes in serum (~50 ng/mL; trace amounts), yet biologically extremely potent
  • Half-life in serum: ~2 days; but IgE bound to mast cells/basophils has a half-life of weeks-months
  • Fc receptor: Binds with extremely high affinity (Kd ~10−10 M) to FcεRI on mast cells and basophils. This pre-arming of mast cells with IgE is a unique feature — the cells are sensitized BEFORE re-exposure to antigen.
  • Mechanism of allergy: (1) First exposure → IgE produced → binds FcεRI on mast cells. (2) Re-exposure → allergen cross-links two IgE molecules on the mast cell surface → mast cell degranulation → release of histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, cytokines → allergic symptoms.
  • Protective role: IgE is critically important for immunity against parasites (helminths). IgE-coated parasites trigger eosinophil ADCC (via FcεRII on eosinophils), killing the parasite. Elevated IgE is a hallmark of parasitic infections.
  • Class switching to IgE: Requires IL-4 (and IL-13) signaling; enhanced by IL-5, IL-9. Blocked by IFN-γ. This is why Th2 cytokine responses drive allergy and anti-parasite immunity.

Complement Activation — Classical Pathway

Definition — Complement System

The complement system is a cascade of ~30 serum proteins that, when activated, amplifies the immune response and directly destroys pathogens. The classical pathway is activated by antigen–antibody complexes (specifically IgM and IgG). Both IgM and IgG are mentioned in the notes as activating complement via this pathway.

The classical pathway overview in the context of Ig function:

  1. Antibody binds antigen: IgM (pentamer) or IgG binds to antigens on the surface of a pathogen (e.g., bacterial cell wall).
  2. C1q recognition: The first complement component, C1q, binds to the Fc region of antibody–antigen complexes. IgM is much more efficient than IgG because a single IgM pentamer presents multiple Fc regions simultaneously, allowing C1q binding with one molecule. For IgG, at least two IgG molecules in close proximity are needed for C1q binding.
  3. C1 complex activation: C1q binding activates C1r → C1s (serine proteases), forming the active C1 complex.
  4. C4 and C2 cleavage: C1s cleaves C4 → C4a + C4b; and C2 → C2a + C2b. C4b and C2a associate to form the C3 convertase (C4b2a).
  5. C3 amplification: C3 convertase cleaves hundreds of C3 molecules → C3a (anaphylatoxin) + C3b (opsonin). C3b deposits on the pathogen surface → opsonization for phagocytosis.
  6. Membrane Attack Complex (MAC): C3b incorporation into C5 convertase → C5a (potent anaphylatoxin and chemoattractant) + C5b → C5b triggers assembly of C6, C7, C8, C9 → MAC (C5b-9) inserts into pathogen membrane → osmotic lysis.
Complement Product Type Function
C3b Opsonin Coats pathogen → recognized by CR1 on phagocytes → enhanced phagocytosis
C3a / C5a Anaphylatoxins Bind receptors on mast cells → degranulation → inflammation; C5a also a potent chemotactic factor for neutrophils
MAC (C5b-9) Pore-forming Creates holes in gram-negative bacterial membranes → osmotic lysis of bacteria
IgM vs IgG Efficiency in Complement Activation

The notes emphasize that both IgM and IgG activate complement via the classical pathway, but IgM does so with much higher efficiency:

  • IgM: A single pentameric IgM molecule, when antigen-bound, exposes multiple Fc regions → C1q can bind with high avidity to a single IgM pentamer. Hence, even low concentrations of IgM early in the immune response can effectively activate complement.
  • IgG: Requires two IgG molecules to be spatially adjacent on the antigen surface to allow C1q binding (C1q has 6 globular heads that need to bridge adjacent Fc regions). IgG3 > IgG1 > IgG2 in classical pathway activation. IgG4 does not activate classical complement.
Section Summary — Complement
  • Both IgM and IgG activate complement via the classical pathway
  • IgM is more efficient (single pentamer sufficient); IgG requires two adjacent molecules
  • Classical pathway key steps: C1q binds Fc → C1 activates → C3 convertase → C3b (opsonin) + C3a (inflammation) → MAC (lysis)
  • IgA activates complement only via alternative/lectin pathways; IgD and IgE do not activate complement

CHEM-10 · Lecture 10 — Immunoglobulin Classes — Everything That Matters

🔬 Ig Biology — Core Concepts

  • Ig = transmembrane (BCR) on B cells OR secreted by plasma cells
  • Two functions: Antigen recognition (Fab) + Effector function (Fc)
  • Fab = VH + VL + CH1 + CL; Fc = CH2 + CH3 (±CH4)
  • Specificity determined by CDRs in V regions → V(D)J recombination
  • BCR = membrane Ig + Igα/Igβ signal subunits
  • Plasma cell = terminally differentiated B cell; secretes antibody

🔄 Class Switching

  • IgM first → IgG/IgA/IgE later — same antigen specificity
  • V(D)J recombination = creates CDR diversity (RAG1/2)
  • Class switch recombination = changes heavy chain constant region (AID)
  • Requires: CD40–CD40L interaction + cytokines (IL-4→IgE; TGF-β→IgA)
  • HIGM: no CD40L → no switching → elevated IgM, absent IgG/IgA

🔵 IgM — Must-Know Points

  • Pentamer — 5 monomers + J chain — ~900 kDa
  • 10 antigen-binding sites → best agglutinator
  • Restricted to intravascular space
  • First antibody in primary response; declines faster than IgG
  • Very efficient complement activator (classical pathway)
  • IgM serology = acute infection marker

🟢 IgG — Must-Know Points

  • Most abundant Ig in serum (75–80%)
  • Monomer; 2 antigen-binding sites; ~150 kDa
  • Half-life ~23 days (longest) — FcRn recycling
  • ONLY Ig crossing placenta → neonatal passive immunity
  • Both intravascular and extravascular distribution
  • 4 subclasses: IgG1 (most), IgG3 (best complement), IgG2 (polysaccharides), IgG4 (anti-inflammatory)

⚔️ ADCC Mechanism

  • IgG coats target cell surface antigens
  • NK cell FcγRIII (CD16) binds Fc region of bound IgG
  • NK-target cell close contact established
  • NK releases perforin + granzymes
  • Target cell undergoes apoptosis
  • NK cell survives; kills multiple targets

🩺 Clinical — HIGM Syndrome

  • Hyper-IgM Syndrome (HIGM) — primary immunodeficiency
  • Cause: absent CD40L on T cells (most common, X-linked) or CD40 on B cells
  • Result: No class switch → elevated IgM + absent IgG, IgA, IgE
  • Clinical: Recurrent bacterial infections (pneumococcus, H. influenzae)
  • Also: Pneumocystis, Cryptosporidium infections
  • Treat: IVIG replacement + prophylactic antibiotics

🧬 Complement Activation (Classical)

  • IgM or IgG–Ag complex → C1q binds Fc
  • IgM >> IgG in efficiency (single pentamer sufficient)
  • C1→C4→C2→C3 convertase→C3b + C3a
  • C3b = opsonin (enhances phagocytosis)
  • C5a = chemotaxis for neutrophils + anaphylatoxin
  • MAC = membrane lysis of gram-negative bacteria

📍 Isotype Location Summary

  • IgM → Intravascular (large size)
  • IgG → Intravascular + interstitial tissues
  • Secretory IgA → Mucosal surfaces (gut, respiratory, GU)
  • IgD → Naive B cell surface (BCR co-receptor)
  • IgE → Bound to mast cells/basophils in tissues
  • sIgA first defense vs influenza, HIV at mucosae

🎯 Fc Receptor-Expressing Cells

  • Monocytes → FcγRI, FcγRII, FcγRIII
  • Macrophages → FcγRI, FcγRII, FcγRIII
  • Dendritic cells → FcγRII, FcγRIII
  • Neutrophils → FcγRIII, FcγRII
  • NK cells → FcγRIII (CD16) → ADCC
  • Mast cells/Basophils → FcεRI (IgE receptor)

🔑 Key Comparisons to Know

  • IgM = early, vascular, 10-site, pentamer, best complement
  • IgG = late, everywhere, 2-site, monomer, placenta, ADCC
  • IgM serology → acute; IgG serology → past/vaccinated
  • Fab = specificity; Fc = function (effector)
  • V(D)J = antigen diversity; CSR = class (isotype) change
  • ADCC = antibody bridges NK cell to target → apoptosis
