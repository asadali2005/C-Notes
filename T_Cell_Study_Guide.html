T Cells — Overview & Classification

T cells (T lymphocytes) are a cornerstone of the adaptive immune system. Unlike B cells, which produce antibodies (humoral immunity), T cells mediate cell-mediated immunity — a direct, cell-to-cell form of defense. They develop in the thymus (hence "T"), where they acquire their identity and function.

📖 Core Definition
T cells are lymphocytes that mature in the thymus and recognize antigens only when displayed on the surface of other cells (in association with MHC proteins). They cannot recognize free antigens — this is the fundamental distinction from B cells.

Two Functional Categories

All T-cell functions can be grouped into two broad categories:

🔧 Regulatory Functions

Orchestrating and amplifying the immune response. Mediated primarily by CD4+ helper T cells through interleukin secretion.

  • ◆ Activate B cells → antibody production
  • ◆ Activate CD8 cytotoxic T cells
  • ◆ Enhance macrophage killing ability
  • ◆ Mediate delayed hypersensitivity
⚔️ Effector Functions

Direct destruction of abnormal or infected cells. Carried out primarily by CD8+ cytotoxic T cells.

  • ◆ Kill virus-infected cells
  • ◆ Kill tumor cells
  • ◆ Kill allograft (transplanted) cells
  • ◆ Induce apoptosis in target cells
💡 Key Insight
CD4 cells direct the immune response; CD8 cells execute it. The analogy: CD4 = generals issuing orders; CD8 = soldiers carrying out the attack.

Section Summary

  • T cells perform regulatory (CD4+) and effector (CD8+) functions
  • Regulatory functions use interleukin signaling to amplify immunity
  • Effector functions involve direct killing of infected/abnormal cells
  • Both cell types express CD3 as a shared surface marker
02

Origin, Differentiation & Surface Markers

Thymic Development

T-cell progenitors (precursor cells from bone marrow) migrate to the thymus, where they undergo differentiation. A critical site of this process is within the outer cortical epithelial cells, also called "nurse cells", which physically support and guide developing T cells.

📖 Thymic Hormones
  • Thymosins — peptide hormones secreted by thymic epithelial cells that promote T-cell differentiation
  • Thymopoietins — another class of thymic hormones with similar roles in T-cell maturation

Under the influence of these hormones, progenitors differentiate into mature T-cell subpopulations with distinct surface glycoproteins.

Key Surface Glycoproteins

Protein Found On Function
CD3 ALL T cells Signal transduction complex associated with TCR; transmits signal from outside to inside the cell when antigen is bound
CD4 Helper T cells only Binds Class II MHC proteins on APCs; stabilizes TCR–MHC interaction; defines the helper subset
CD8 Cytotoxic T cells only Binds Class I MHC proteins on target cells; stabilizes TCR–MHC interaction; defines the cytotoxic subset
TCR (T-Cell Receptor) ALL T cells Antigen-specific receptor; always associated with CD3; recognizes antigen–MHC complex
💡 Mutual Exclusivity
Mature T cells have either CD4 or CD8 — never both. During thymic development, immature thymocytes transiently express both (double-positive stage), but mature cells commit to one lineage. This ensures each T cell has a specific, defined role.

CD3 Complex — The Signal Transmitter

The CD3 complex consists of five transmembrane proteins. It is always found on the T-cell surface in association with the TCR. Its critical role is intracellular signal transduction — when the TCR binds to an antigen–MHC complex, CD3 relays this information from the outside of the cell to the inside, triggering activation cascades.

⚠️ Common Mistake
The TCR recognizes antigen, but CD3 transmits the signal. TCR alone cannot signal — it needs CD3. Think of TCR as the "lock" and CD3 as the "alarm" that goes off when the key fits.

Section Summary

  • T-cell progenitors differentiate in the thymus under thymosins and thymopoietins
  • Nurse cells (cortical epithelial cells) guide this differentiation
  • All T cells express CD3 (signal transmitter) and TCR (antigen recognition)
  • CD4 or CD8 expression is mutually exclusive on mature T cells
  • CD3 = 5-protein complex essential for signaling when TCR is occupied
03

CD4+ Helper T Cells

CD4+ T cells are the master coordinators of the immune response. Without them, neither B cells nor CD8 cells function optimally. They produce interleukins that amplify, direct, and shape the entire immune response.

📖 CD4+ T Cell — Distribution
CD4+ cells make up approximately 65% of peripheral T cells. They predominate in the thymic medulla, tonsils, and blood.

Three Core Helper Functions

  1. Help B cells develop into antibody-producing plasma cells (via IL-4 and IL-5 from Th-2 subpopulation)
  2. Help CD8 T cells become activated cytotoxic T cells (via IL-2 from Th-1 subpopulation)
  3. Help macrophages — enhance their killing ability via gamma interferon (from Th-1 subpopulation)

The Two Subpopulations of CD4 Cells

CD4+ cells are not a monolithic population. They are divided into at least two major functional subsets:

Subpopulation Primary Cytokines Produced Main Functions
Th-1 (T-helper 1) IL-2, Gamma Interferon (IFN-γ) (1) Activate cytotoxic T cells
(2) Initiate delayed hypersensitivity response
(3) Activate macrophages
Th-2 (T-helper 2) IL-4, IL-5 (1) Activate B cells → antibody production
(2) Promote IgE production (especially with IL-4)
(3) Critical for anti-parasite responses
💡 Why Two Subpopulations?
The immune system needs to match its response to the threat. Th-1 responses are optimal against intracellular pathogens (viruses, bacteria like TB) where cell-mediated killing is needed. Th-2 responses are optimal against extracellular parasites and allergens where antibody-mediated clearance and IgE are key. Th-1 and Th-2 cells actually inhibit each other, creating a balanced, context-appropriate response.

Key Interleukins Produced by CD4+ Cells

Interleukin Source (CD4 subtype) Target Effect
IL-2 Th-1 CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (including itself) Activates and promotes proliferation of both helper and cytotoxic T cells (autocrine and paracrine); essential for T-cell expansion
IL-4 Th-2 B cells Promotes B-cell activation and differentiation; especially important for class switching to IgE (role in allergy and anti-parasitic immunity)
IL-5 Th-2 B cells, Eosinophils B-cell growth and differentiation; activates eosinophils (important in parasite defense and allergy)
IFN-γ (Gamma Interferon) Th-1 Macrophages Activates macrophages, dramatically enhancing their ability to kill intracellular microbes; key in delayed hypersensitivity
🧠 Memory Aid
  • IL-2 = "Turns On T cells" (2 = Two types of T cells activated)
  • IL-4 = "IgE for Allergies" (4th letter = 4-ward allergies to IgE)
  • IL-5 = "Five Eosinophils" (eosinophilia)
  • Gamma IFN = "Gets Macrophages Inflamed" (macrophage activation)

Section Summary

  • CD4+ cells = 65% of peripheral T cells; found in thymic medulla, tonsils, blood
  • They help B cells (plasma cell differentiation), CD8 cells (activation), and macrophages (killing)
  • Th-1 → IL-2 + IFN-γ → cell-mediated immunity and macrophage activation
  • Th-2 → IL-4 + IL-5 → humoral (antibody) immunity, IgE, anti-parasite
  • IL-2 activates both CD4 and CD8 T cells (autocrine + paracrine)
04

CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells

CD8+ T cells are the immune system's professional killers. Once activated, they hunt down and eliminate cells that display abnormal intracellular contents — whether from viral infection, malignant transformation, or foreign grafted tissue.

📖 CD8+ T Cell — Distribution
CD8+ cells make up approximately 35% of peripheral T cells. They predominate in human bone marrow and gut lymphoid tissue (GALT — gut-associated lymphoid tissue).

Targets of CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells

🎯 What They Kill
  • Virus-infected cells — the primary and most important target
  • Tumor cells — cells expressing abnormal or mutant proteins
  • Allograft cells — foreign transplanted tissue expressing non-self MHC

Two Killing Mechanisms

CD8+ cells can eliminate targets through two distinct pathways:

💣 Mechanism 1: Perforins

CD8 cells release perforins — pore-forming proteins that insert into the target cell's lipid bilayer and create cylindrical holes. This disrupts the cell membrane integrity, causing osmotic lysis (water rushes in, cell bursts and dies).

Analogous to antibody complement system (MAC), but delivered directly by a T cell.
🍂 Mechanism 2: Apoptosis

CD8 cells trigger programmed cell death (apoptosis) in the target cell. This occurs via Fas–FasL interaction (CD95 pathway) or granzyme B (serine protease released by CD8 cells that activates caspase cascades).

Apoptosis is "clean" — no inflammatory debris is released, unlike necrosis.
💡 Why Two Mechanisms?
Perforins cause rapid, membrane-destroying necrosis — useful when speed matters. Apoptosis is a controlled "self-destruct" program that prevents release of intracellular contents (including viral particles), minimizing collateral damage and inflammation. CD8 cells can deploy either or both depending on context.

Section Summary

  • CD8+ cells = 35% of peripheral T cells; predominate in bone marrow and gut
  • Kill virus-infected cells, tumor cells, and allograft cells
  • Killing mechanism 1: Perforins → membrane pores → osmotic lysis
  • Killing mechanism 2: Apoptosis (programmed cell death) via granzymes/Fas-FasL
  • Both mechanisms require direct cell-to-cell contact and MHC Class I recognition

T Cell Activation — Core Principles

T cells do not activate randomly. A highly specific and controlled process ensures that only the right T cells respond to the right threats — a central principle of adaptive immunity called clonal selection.

The Role of Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs)

📖 Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs)
APCs are specialized cells that process antigens and display them on their surface in association with MHC proteins. Key APCs include:
  • Dendritic cells — the most potent APCs; especially effective at initiating T-cell responses
  • Macrophages — process extracellular antigens and activate helper T cells
  • B cells — present antigens to helper T cells to receive help in return
APCs have both Class I and Class II MHC proteins on their surface, allowing them to activate both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells.

MHC–CD Interaction — The Fundamental Rule

T Cell Type Surface Protein Recognizes MHC Class Context
CD4+ Helper T cell CD4 Class II MHC Antigen-presenting cells (APCs); responds to extracellular antigens
CD8+ Cytotoxic T cell CD8 Class I MHC Any nucleated cell; responds to intracellular antigens (viruses, tumor proteins)
🧠 The Rule of Eight — Never Forget This
CD4 × Class II = 4 × 2 = 8
CD8 × Class I  = 8 × 1 = 8

Both products equal 8 — the rule that ensures you never mix up which CD marker goes with which MHC class.

Why This MHC Restriction Matters

MHC restriction ensures appropriate immune responses:

  • ▸Class II MHC is only on APCs and immune cells. CD4 cells interacting with Class II ensures that helper T cells only engage with cells presenting processed pathogens — preventing accidental attack of normal body cells.
  • ▸Class I MHC is on virtually ALL nucleated cells. CD8 cells interacting with Class I ensures that cytotoxic T cells can surveil and kill ANY infected cell, not just immune cells.

Section Summary

  • APCs (dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells) present antigens via MHC proteins
  • APCs express both Class I and Class II MHC simultaneously
  • CD4 binds Class II MHC (4 × 2 = 8)
  • CD8 binds Class I MHC (8 × 1 = 8) — the "Rule of Eight"
  • This MHC restriction is the basis of T-cell specificity and safety

Activation of Helper T Cells (CD4+)

The activation of helper T cells is a multi-step, spatially coordinated process. It begins when a pathogen breaches a barrier and ends with a fully activated helper T cell orchestrating the immune response.

The CCR7 Migration Mechanism

This is an elegant solution to a spatial problem: APCs are typically under epithelial surfaces (skin, gut, lung), while T cells are primarily in lymph nodes. How do they meet?

🔑 CCR7 — The Navigation System
  • T cells in lymph nodes continuously produce the chemokine CCR7 (C-C chemokine receptor type 7), creating a gradient.
  • After ingesting a microbe, the APC (e.g., dendritic cell) upregulates a receptor for the CCR7 chemokine.
  • The dendritic cell then follows the ascending CCR7 gradient from the epithelium through lymphatic vessels to the lymph node.
  • There, it presents antigen to the waiting T cells.

Think of CCR7 as a beacon emitted by T cells, guiding dendritic cells to their location.

Section Summary

  • APC ingests pathogen → cleaves protein → associates with Class II MHC → surface display
  • APC migrates to lymph node following CCR7 chemokine gradient
  • In lymph node: antigen–Class II MHC binds TCR of CD4+ T cell
  • CD4 protein stabilizes the APC–T cell interaction
  • Costimulation (B7–CD28) is the second essential signal → IL-2 production → full activation

Activation of Cytotoxic T Cells (CD8+)

CD8+ T cell activation requires recognition of antigen–Class I MHC complexes. There are two main scenarios by which this occurs:

Pathway 1: Direct Viral Presentation (Classical)

When an APC itself becomes infected with a virus:

Pathway 1 — APC Is Infected
Virus infects APC directly
↓
Viral proteins synthesized inside APC cytoplasm
↓
Viral peptides associate with Class I MHC proteins
Intracellular pathway — proteosomes degrade viral proteins
↓
Complex transported to APC surface
↓
CD8 TCR recognizes viral antigen–Class I MHC
↓
CD8 protein stabilizes interaction
↓
IL-2 (from activated CD4+ cells) → CD8 fully activated

Pathway 2: Cross-Presentation

📖 Cross-Presentation
Cross-presentation is the ability of APCs (particularly dendritic cells) to acquire antigens from dying virus-infected cells and present them on Class I MHC proteins — even though the APC itself was not infected.
  • APC ingests pieces of a dying virus-infected cell
  • Viral antigens from the ingested material are processed and loaded onto Class I MHC
  • This cross-presented antigen activates CD8+ T cells

Why important? Not all viruses infect APCs directly. Cross-presentation allows CD8 cells to be activated even against viruses that infect non-APC cells.

Pathway 3: Direct Non-APC Presentation (Effector Phase)

Once CD8+ T cells are activated, they can recognize and kill any infected non-APC cell:

🔑 How Infected Non-APC Cells Are Targeted
  • Inside a virus-infected cell (e.g., a muscle cell, liver cell), newly synthesized viral peptides associate with Class I MHC proteins
  • This complex is transported to the cell surface
  • The viral antigen is now displayed on the surface, recognized by the TCR of a circulating CD8+ cytotoxic T cell
  • The CD8 protein on the T cell binds Class I MHC on the infected cell, stabilizing the interaction
  • The CD8 cell then kills the infected cell via perforins or apoptosis

Every nucleated cell in the body is thus a potential "suspect" that CD8 cells can interrogate via Class I MHC display.

Section Summary

  • Pathway 1: APC infected → viral proteins → Class I MHC → CD8 activation
  • Pathway 2: Cross-presentation — APC ingests dying infected cells → presents viral antigen via Class I MHC to CD8 cells
  • Pathway 3 (effector): Any infected nucleated cell displays antigen–Class I MHC → CD8 kills it
  • CD8 protein always binds Class I MHC to stabilize the killing synapse
  • IL-2 (from helper T cells) is needed for full CD8 activation

Costimulation — The Two-Signal Model

One of the most important safety mechanisms in the immune system is the requirement for two independent signals to activate a T cell. This prevents accidental activation against self-antigens.

📖 Two-Signal Model of T Cell Activation
A T cell requires two signals simultaneously to become fully activated:
  • Signal 1 — Antigen recognition: TCR binds the antigen–MHC complex
  • Signal 2 (Costimulatory) — B7 protein on APC binds CD28 protein on the helper T cell
Both signals must occur together. Signal 1 alone leads to anergy, not activation.

Signal 1 — Antigen Recognition

The TCR on the T cell, which is specific for a particular antigen epitope, binds the antigen–MHC complex on the APC surface. This is highly specific — only T cells with the matching TCR will respond. The CD4 or CD8 co-receptor then binds to the MHC protein to stabilize this interaction.

Signal 2 — B7–CD28 Costimulation

🔑 The Costimulatory Signal
  • B7 protein is on the APC surface (upregulated during infection/inflammation)
  • CD28 protein is on the helper T cell surface
  • B7 (APC) ↔ CD28 (T cell) interaction = the costimulatory signal
  • When this second signal occurs alongside Signal 1: the T cell is activated → produces IL-2
  • IL-2 is then critical for the T cell to perform its regulatory, effector, and memory functions

What Happens Without Costimulation — Anergy

⚠️ Anergy — Immune Paralysis
If Signal 1 (TCR–antigen–MHC) occurs without Signal 2 (B7–CD28), the T cell does not activate. Instead, it enters a state of anergy — a long-lived state of functional unresponsiveness.

Clinical Importance: Anergy is how the immune system prevents autoimmunity. Self-antigens that are presented to T cells without costimulation (which requires an inflammatory context) will lead to anergy instead of activation, ensuring tolerance to self.
Two-Signal Model — Outcomes
✅
Signal 1 + Signal 2

AND
B7 binds CD28

→ Full T cell Activation
→ IL-2 Production
→ Effector + Memory T cells
❌

💡 Clinical Application — Cancer Immunotherapy
Tumor cells often downregulate B7 expression, thus avoiding the costimulatory signal and inducing anergy in T cells (immune evasion). Modern cancer immunotherapies (e.g., checkpoint inhibitors like anti-CTLA-4, anti-PD-1) work partly by restoring this costimulatory balance and reinvigorating anergic T cells.

Section Summary

  • Two signals are required: (1) TCR–antigen–MHC, (2) B7 (APC) – CD28 (T cell)
  • Signal 1 alone → Anergy (unresponsiveness); both signals → Activation + IL-2 production
  • IL-2 is the critical product that enables regulatory, effector, and memory functions
  • Anergy is a key mechanism of self-tolerance and prevention of autoimmunity

CTLA-4 & T Cell Deactivation (Homeostasis)

The immune system must be capable of turning itself off as well as on. After a pathogen is cleared, continued T-cell activation would be harmful (autoimmunity, tissue damage). The CTLA-4 molecule is central to this "off switch."

📖 CTLA-4 (Cytotoxic T Lymphocyte Antigen-4)
CTLA-4 is an inhibitory receptor on T cells that appears on the surface after the T cell has been activated. It binds to B7 on the APC with higher affinity than CD28, displacing CD28 and blocking the costimulatory signal.

Mechanism of CTLA-4 Action

CTLA-4 Feedback Inhibition Pathway
T cell is activated (Signal 1 + Signal 2)
↓
IL-2 produced; T cell proliferates and functions
↓
CTLA-4 protein appears on T cell surface
Upregulated as a feedback response to activation
↓
CTLA-4 binds B7 (displacing CD28) with higher affinity
↓
CD28 can no longer bind B7 → costimulatory signal blocked
↓
IL-2 synthesis inhibited
↓
T cell returns to quiescent (resting) state
🔑 Why CTLA-4 Binds Better Than CD28
CTLA-4 binds B7 with approximately 10–20× higher affinity than CD28. This ensures that once CTLA-4 appears, it outcompetes CD28 and reliably shuts off the costimulatory signal. This is a critical safety mechanism for T-cell homeostasis.

Consequence of CTLA-4 Deficiency — Autoimmunity

⚠️ CTLA-4 Mutation → Autoimmunity
Mutant T cells that lack CTLA-4 cannot be deactivated. Without the CTLA-4 brake, T cells remain perpetually activated and attack the body's own tissues, causing autoimmune reactions.

Experimentally, CTLA-4 knockout mice develop severe, fatal multi-organ autoimmunity shortly after birth — dramatically demonstrating how essential this molecule is for self-tolerance.

CTLA-4 in Clinical Medicine — Checkpoint Inhibitors

💡 Cancer Immunotherapy Connection
Ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4 antibody) is a FDA-approved cancer drug that blocks CTLA-4, preventing it from turning off T cells. By keeping T cells "on," the drug allows them to continue attacking tumor cells. This represents a landmark application of basic CTLA-4 biology to clinical cancer treatment.

  • CTLA-4 appears on T-cell surface after activation (not before)
  • Displaces CD28 by binding B7 with higher affinity → blocks costimulatory signal
  • Result: IL-2 synthesis stops → T cell returns to quiescent state
  • CTLA-4 is essential for T-cell homeostasis and self-tolerance
  • CTLA-4 absence → uncontrolled T cell activation → autoimmunity
  • Anti-CTLA-4 drugs (ipilimumab) exploit this in cancer therapy

⚡ Master Revision Sheet

CD4+ Helper T Cells

  • 65% of peripheral T cells
  • Thymic medulla, tonsils, blood
  • Recognizes Class II MHC (4×2=8)
  • Th-1: IL-2 + IFN-γ → cytotoxic cells + macrophages
  • Th-2: IL-4 + IL-5 → B cells + IgE + eosinophils
  • IL-2 activates both CD4 and CD8
  • Primary HIV target (CD4 = HIV co-receptor)

CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells

  • 35% of peripheral T cells
  • Bone marrow + gut lymphoid tissue
  • Recognizes Class I MHC (8×1=8)
  • Kill: virus-infected, tumor, allograft cells
  • Kill via: perforins (membrane lysis)
  • Kill via: apoptosis (programmed cell death)
  • Needs IL-2 from CD4 for full activation

Surface Markers (All T Cells)

  • CD3 — all T cells; 5 transmembrane proteins; signal transduction
  • TCR — all T cells; antigen recognition; paired with CD3
  • CD4 OR CD8 — mutually exclusive on mature T cells
  • CD28 — receptor for B7; delivers costimulatory signal
  • CTLA-4 — appears post-activation; turns off T cells

Activation Requirements

  • Signal 1: TCR binds antigen–MHC complex
  • Signal 2: CD28 binds B7 (costimulation)
  • Both signals → IL-2 → full activation
  • Signal 1 only → Anergy (unresponsiveness)
  • APC migrates to lymph node via CCR7 gradient
  • CTLA-4 displaces CD28 → deactivation

Key Interleukins

  • IL-2 → activates CD4 + CD8 T cells (autocrine/paracrine)
  • IL-4 → B cells + IgE class switching (Th-2)
  • IL-5 → B cells + eosinophils (Th-2)
  • IFN-γ → macrophage activation (Th-1)
  • IL-4 + IL-5 → B cell help (Table 58-3)

CD8 Activation Pathways

  • Pathway 1: APC infected → viral proteins → Class I MHC → CD8
  • Pathway 2: Cross-presentation (APC ingests dying infected cell)
  • Pathway 3: Any infected cell displays Class I + viral antigen
  • CD8 protein binds Class I MHC (stabilizes contact)
  • IL-2 from CD4 cells needed for full CD8 activation

CTLA-4 vs CD28 — Quick Comparison

  • Both bind B7 on APC
  • CD28: present always; activates T cell (IL-2)
  • CTLA-4: appears after activation; inhibits T cell
  • CTLA-4 has higher affinity for B7 (displaces CD28)
  • CTLA-4 absent → autoimmunity
  • Anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab) → cancer immunotherapy

Thymic Development

  • Progenitors from bone marrow → thymus
  • Nurse cells (cortical epithelial cells) support development
  • Thymosins + Thymopoietins → differentiation
  • Mature T cells: CD4 OR CD8 (never both)
  • All T cells gain CD3 + TCR in thymus
The Rule of Eight — Never Forget
CD4 × Class II = 4 × 2 = 8   →   Helper T cells interact with Class II MHC
CD8 × Class I  = 8 × 1 = 8   →   Cytotoxic T cells interact with Class I MHC
