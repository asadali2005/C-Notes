Section 1

Gel Electrophoresis of DNA

↑ Top
📖 Definition
Gel electrophoresis is a laboratory technique used to separate DNA (or RNA/protein) fragments by size using an electrical current. It can detect the presence of DNA in a sample and determine the number of nucleotides in each fragment.

Core Purpose — What Does It Tell Us?

  • Presence of DNA: Confirms whether a sample contains DNA at all.
  • Fragment size: Determines how many nucleotides a fragment contains by comparing it to a DNA ladder (standard).
  • Number of distinct fragments: Each band in a gel represents a distinct-sized population of DNA fragments.

The Physical Setup

The sample containing DNA fragments is placed into wells at one end of a gel and forced through it by an electrical current. The gel acts as a molecular sieve — a matrix with uniformly sized pores that DNA molecules must squeeze through.

✅ Key Analogy
Think of the gel as a dense forest. Small animals (short DNA fragments) can dart through easily and travel far. Large animals (long DNA fragments) get stuck between trees and barely move. The distance travelled = inversely proportional to fragment size.

Why Does DNA Move? — The Electrical Basis

DNA carries a net negative charge because of its phosphate backbone. Each phosphate group in the sugar-phosphate backbone is negatively charged at physiological pH. When placed in an electric field:

  • DNA migrates from the negative electrode (cathode/−) toward the positive electrode (anode/+).
  • This is why sample wells are always placed at the negative end.
⚡ Important — SAGE Setup
The most common form of DNA gel electrophoresis is Submerged Agarose Gel Electrophoresis (SAGE) — the gel is completely submerged in buffer solution. This ensures consistent electrical conductivity across the entire gel.

The buffer serves two functions: (1) conducts electricity, and (2) maintains stable pH so that DNA charge remains constant throughout the run.

📌 Section Summary

  • Gel electrophoresis separates DNA by size using an electric current through a gel sieve.
  • DNA is negatively charged (phosphate backbone) → moves toward the positive electrode.
  • Smaller fragments travel farther; larger fragments move less.
  • The SAGE technique (submerged agarose) is standard for DNA work.

Section 2

How DNA Gel Electrophoresis Works

↑ Top

The Mechanism — Step by Step

1
Gel Preparation

Agarose or polyacrylamide is melted, poured into a casting tray with a comb, and allowed to solidify. When the comb is removed, it leaves behind small rectangular wells where samples are loaded.

2
Sample Loading

DNA samples are mixed with a loading dye (e.g., bromophenol blue) and loaded into wells. The dye adds density so samples sink into wells, and it provides a visible front to track the progress of electrophoresis.

3
Electrophoresis Run

A potential difference (voltage) is applied across the gel. DNA fragments, being negatively charged, migrate toward the positive electrode. Electrophoresis continues until the marker dye (bromophenol blue) reaches the end of the gel.

4
Size-based Separation

Because the gel has uniform pore sizes:

  • Longer fragments: more nucleotides → larger molecular weight → larger physical size → trapped by gel pores → travel shorter distance (stay near top).
  • Shorter fragments: fewer nucleotides → smaller size → pass through pores easily → travel farther (near bottom).
  • Intermediate fragments: settle roughly in the middle.
5
Visualization & Sizing

After the run, the gel is stained with ethidium bromide (EtBr) and examined under UV light. DNA fragments appear as orange fluorescent bands. Fragment sizes are estimated by comparing their position to a DNA ladder (kilobase ladder) — a known mixture of DNA fragments of defined sizes run in an adjacent lane.

Students often assume larger DNA fragments travel farther. This is wrong! Larger fragments are impeded by the gel matrix and stay near the wells. Smaller fragments travel the greatest distance.

  • Negatively charged DNA moves toward the positive electrode.
  • Gel pores act as a sieve — larger fragments = slower migration = stays near wells.
  • Bromophenol blue dye tracks progress; ethidium bromide stains DNA for UV visualization.
  • Fragment size is read by comparing to a DNA ladder in an adjacent lane.

Section 3

Gel Types: Agarose vs Polyacrylamide

↑ Top

Two gel types are routinely used in electrophoresis. The choice depends on the size of the molecules being separated.

🌿 Agarose Gel

  • Extracted from seaweed (algae)
  • Purchased as a dry powder
  • Dissolved by melting in buffer at appropriate concentration (typically 0.3–2.0% w/v)
  • On cooling, sets to form a solid gel
  • Large pores → used to separate large molecules
  • Used for nucleic acids, large proteins, protein complexes
  • Run as SAGE (submerged agarose gel electrophoresis)
  • Relatively electrically neutral

🔬 Polyacrylamide Gel (PAGE)

  • Polymer of acrylamide and bis-acrylamide
  • Smaller pores than agarose
  • Used to separate small nucleic acid molecules and most proteins
  • Applications: DNA sequencing gels, protein analysis (SDS-PAGE)
  • Useful separation range: 1 to 1000 bp (depending on % used)
  • Relatively electrically neutral
  • Higher resolution for smaller molecules

Why Are Both Gels Electrically Neutral?

Both agarose and polyacrylamide are electrically neutral materials — they do not carry a charge themselves. This is essential so that the gel matrix does not interfere with the migration of charged molecules. The only factor driving migration should be the applied electric field and the size of the molecule.

✅ Rule of Thumb
  • Big molecules (large DNA, large proteins): Use low-percentage agarose (large pores)
  • Small molecules (proteins, oligonucleotides): Use polyacrylamide (small pores)

How Agarose Concentration Affects Separation

Higher agarose concentration = smaller pores = better resolution of smaller fragments. Lower concentration = larger pores = better for separating large fragments. The same logic applies to polyacrylamide percentage.

📌 Section Summary

  • Agarose: from seaweed, large pores, 0.3–2.0%, used for large nucleic acids and proteins.
  • Polyacrylamide: acrylamide polymer, small pores, used for proteins and small nucleic acids.
  • Both gels are electrically neutral — only the molecule's charge and size drive separation.

Section 4

Complete Procedure & Visualization

↑ Top

Step-by-Step Protocol for Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

1
Prepare the Agarose Gel

Weigh out the appropriate amount of agarose powder and dissolve it by melting in electrophoresis buffer (e.g., TAE or TBE buffer) at the desired concentration (e.g., 1% for general DNA work).

2
Add Ethidium Bromide & Mix

After cooling slightly (to ~60°C so it doesn't solidify yet), add ethidium bromide (EtBr) to the molten gel and mix thoroughly. EtBr is an intercalating dye that inserts between the stacked base pairs of DNA and fluoresces orange-pink under UV light.

3
Cast the Gel

Pour the molten agarose into the casting tray with a comb inserted. Allow it to cool and solidify completely. Remove the comb carefully — this leaves behind the sample wells.

4
Add Running Buffer & Load Samples

Place the solidified gel in the electrophoresis tank. Add running buffer until it just covers the gel surface (SAGE technique). Load DNA samples and a DNA size marker (ladder) into the wells using a micropipette.

5
Run the Gel at Constant Voltage

Apply a constant voltage. Maintain until adequate band separation occurs — monitored by watching the bromophenol blue dye front travel through the gel. Stop when the dye reaches the end of the gel.

6
Visualize on UV Light Box & Document

Place the gel on a UV transilluminator. DNA-EtBr complexes fluoresce bright orange/pink under UV light. Photograph or digitally capture the result. Identify fragment sizes by comparing band positions to the ladder lanes.

Ethidium Bromide — How Visualization Works

📖 Ethidium Bromide (EtBr)
EtBr is an intercalating dye — its flat ring structure intercalates (inserts) between adjacent base pairs in the DNA double helix. When bound to DNA and exposed to UV light (~302 nm), the EtBr-DNA complex fluoresces intensely in the orange range (~590 nm).

DNA shows up as orange bands on a purple background when viewed under UV.
⚠️ Safety Warning
Ethidium bromide is a mutagen and suspected carcinogen because it intercalates into cellular DNA. It must always be handled with gloves and disposed of as hazardous waste. Some labs now use safer alternatives (e.g., SYBR Safe).

The DNA Ladder / Marker

A DNA molecular weight marker (ladder) is a pre-mixed set of DNA fragments of known sizes (e.g., 2,000 bp, 4,000 bp, 6,000 bp, 8,000 bp, 10,000 bp). When run alongside unknown samples, each known band serves as a reference point. The size of an unknown band is estimated by interpolation between ladder bands.

📌 Section Summary

  • Protocol: Prepare gel → Add EtBr → Cast → Load samples + ladder → Run at constant voltage → Visualize under UV.
  • Bromophenol blue tracks gel progress; EtBr stains DNA for UV visualization.
  • EtBr intercalates into DNA → orange fluorescence under UV.
  • Fragment sizes are estimated by comparing to the DNA ladder.

✅ Pattern to Remember
  • Lower % concentration = Larger pores = Separates LARGER molecules.
  • Higher % concentration = Smaller pores = Separates SMALLER molecules.

PAGE for Nucleic Acids

Polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) is used for small nucleic acids in applications such as DNA sequencing. Its pore size is smaller than achievable with agarose, enabling resolution of fragments that differ by just a single nucleotide — a prerequisite for DNA sequencing gel analysis.

SDS-PAGE — Protein Gel Electrophoresis

↑ Top
📖 Definition — SDS-PAGE
SDS-PAGE (Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate – Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis) is a technique for separating proteins based solely on their molecular weight (size), by eliminating the effect of their intrinsic charge and tertiary structure.

The Problem with Protein Electrophoresis

Unlike DNA (which has a uniform negative charge/mass ratio due to its regular structure), proteins are more complex:

  • Different proteins have different intrinsic charges at any given pH (due to their unique amino acid composition).
  • Many proteins have multiple subunits held together by various bonds including disulfide bridges.
  • Proteins have complex 3D folded structures (tertiary/quaternary structure) that affect their shape and mobility.

If you run proteins without treatment, they separate based on a combination of size AND charge — making size estimation impossible.

How SDS Solves This Problem

SDS (sodium dodecyl sulphate) is a strong anionic detergent. When proteins are heated with SDS and mercaptoethanol:

1
Denaturation — Unfolding the Protein

Heating with SDS breaks non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic interactions), fully unfolding the protein into a linear polypeptide chain.

2
Reduction of Disulfide Bonds

Mercaptoethanol (β-ME) is a reducing agent that breaks disulfide (–S–S–) bridges between cysteine residues. This separates multi-subunit proteins into individual polypeptide chains.

3
Charge Masking — Creating a Uniform Charge/Mass Ratio

SDS molecules, which are negatively charged, bind proportionally to the mass of the polypeptide chain (approximately 1.4 g SDS per gram of protein). This masks the intrinsic charge of each protein and imparts a uniform negative charge proportional to molecular weight. The charge/mass ratio becomes constant for all proteins.

4
Electrophoresis — Separation by Size Only

Since all proteins now have the same charge/mass ratio, the only variable determining mobility through the polyacrylamide gel is the physical size (molecular weight) of each polypeptide. This is analogous to how DNA separates in agarose.

🧮 The Core Principle of SDS-PAGE
After SDS treatment:

Charge/Mass Ratio = CONSTANT for all proteins
Therefore: Migration Distance ∝ 1 / Molecular Weight

(Smaller proteins travel farther — just like DNA!)

What Can SDS-PAGE Tell Us?

  • Molecular weight of a protein (by comparison to a protein ladder/marker).
  • Purity of a protein preparation (one band = pure; multiple bands = contamination).
  • Number of subunits in a multi-subunit protein (compare native vs. SDS-PAGE patterns).
  • Relative abundance of proteins in a mixture.
⚠️ SDS-PAGE Cannot Determine:
Native charge, native conformation/activity, or the exact number of subunits in quaternary structure (unless reducing and non-reducing conditions are compared side by side).

📌 Section Summary

  • SDS is an anionic detergent — denatures proteins and imparts a uniform negative charge proportional to mass.
  • Mercaptoethanol reduces disulfide bonds, separating subunits.
  • After SDS treatment, all proteins have the same charge/mass ratio → they separate purely by size.
  • PAGE is used (not agarose) because its small pores provide resolution for the protein size range.

Section 7

Southern & Northern Blotting

↑ Top
📖 Definition — Blotting
Blotting is a technique used after gel electrophoresis to detect specific nucleic acid sequences (or proteins) within a complex mixture, using a complementary labelled molecule called a probe.

Why Blotting After Electrophoresis?

Gel electrophoresis separates thousands of DNA fragments by size — but you cannot see which specific band contains your gene of interest just from EtBr staining. Blotting adds a second layer of specificity: after separating by size, you test which band contains your target sequence.

🧬 Southern Blotting (DNA)

  • Detects specific DNA sequences
  • Named after inventor Edwin Southern (1975)
  • DNA is cleaved with restriction enzymes before electrophoresis
  • Uses a labelled DNA or RNA probe complementary to the target
  • Published in J. Mol. Biol. 98:508

🔬 Northern Blotting (RNA)

  • Detects specific RNA sequences
  • A similar procedure to Southern blotting
  • Used to study gene expression (mRNA levels)
  • Named humorously as a counterpart to "Southern"
  • Tells you which tissues express a gene and how much

How Southern Blotting Works — Step by Step

1
Restriction Digestion

Genomic DNA is cut with one or more restriction enzymes into manageable fragments.

2
Gel Electrophoresis

The restriction fragments are separated by agarose gel electrophoresis.

3
Denaturation

The gel is soaked in alkaline solution to denature DNA into single strands. Single-stranded DNA is necessary for hybridization with the probe.

4
Transfer (Blotting) to Membrane

DNA fragments are transferred from the fragile gel to a sturdy nitrocellulose or nylon membrane by capillary action. The blotting apparatus stacks absorbent paper/tissues above the gel, drawing buffer (and DNA) upward by capillary action, transferring DNA fragments from gel → membrane. The spatial pattern (relative positions of bands) is perfectly preserved.

5
Hybridization with Labelled Probe

The membrane is incubated with a labelled probe — a single-stranded DNA or RNA molecule with a sequence complementary to the target. The probe hybridizes (base-pairs) only with the specific fragment containing the target sequence.

6
Detection — Autoradiogram

After washing away unbound probe, the membrane is placed against X-ray film (autoradiography) or detected by chemiluminescence. Only the band where the probe hybridized will appear — revealing the location and size of the target DNA fragment.

✅ Why Blotting Can Detect One Sequence Among Millions
The probe is designed to be complementary to only the target sequence. Even in a complex mixture of millions of DNA fragments, only the target will bind the probe tightly enough to survive the washing steps. This is the power of nucleic acid hybridization (complementary base pairing).

The Blotting Apparatus

The transfer setup includes: a transfer buffer reservoir at the base, a support block, the gel placed on filter paper wick, the nitrocellulose/nylon membrane on top of the gel, stacked filter paper and paper tissues, and a weight to ensure even contact. Transfer buffer is drawn upward through the gel by capillary action, carrying the nucleic acid fragments onto the membrane.

📌 Section Summary

  • Southern blotting = detect specific DNA; Northern blotting = detect specific RNA.
  • Probes are labelled single-stranded nucleic acids that hybridize with complementary sequences.
  • Transfer from gel to nitrocellulose membrane occurs by capillary action.
  • Detection by autoradiography shows which band contains the target sequence.

Polymerase Chain Reaction — Introduction

↑ Top
📖 Definition — PCR
PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) is an in vitro (in a test tube) technique for the exponential amplification of a specific region of DNA whose sequence is known, or which lies between two regions of known sequence. It can generate millions to billions of copies of a target DNA sequence from just a few starting molecules.

Why Is PCR Necessary?

Many molecular biology techniques — sequencing, cloning, forensic analysis — require large amounts of a specific DNA sequence. Natural DNA in a sample is present in tiny quantities. PCR solves this by selectively amplifying only the region of interest, billions of times, in just a few hours.

Section 10

PCR Components — In Detail

↑ Top

1. DNA Template

  • The DNA containing the region to be amplified.
  • Can be genomic DNA, cDNA, plasmid DNA, or even degraded samples (forensic, ancient DNA).
  • The target DNA to be amplified is typically up to 3 kilobases (kb) in standard PCR.
  • Only a tiny amount is needed — theoretically a single molecule is sufficient.

2. Primers

📖 What Are Primers?
Primers are short, single-stranded oligonucleotides that define the start and end points of the DNA region to be amplified. They provide a 3′-OH group for Taq polymerase to extend from.

Key Properties of PCR Primers

  • Two sets are used (a forward primer and a reverse primer — one for each strand).
  • Generally 20–30 nucleotides long (17–30 nt range for effective primers).
  • Synthetically produced (chemically synthesized in a laboratory).
  • Complementary to the 3′ ends of the target DNA on each strand — they flank the region of interest.
  • Not complementary to each other — primers must not bind to each other (this would form primer-dimers, wasting primers and reducing PCR efficiency).

Criteria for Effective Primer Design

✅ Effective Primer Design Rules
  • Length: 17–30 nucleotides (provides sufficient specificity).
  • GC content: ~50% ideal — higher GC increases melting temperature (Tm) and specificity; too high causes hairpins.
  • No long homopolymer runs (e.g., AAAAAAA) — these reduce specificity and can cause slippage.
  • No significant secondary structure — primers that fold back on themselves (hairpins) cannot bind template efficiently.
  • No complementarity between the two primers — prevents primer-dimer formation, which wastes primers and reduces yield.
⚠️ What Happens with Bad Primers?
  • Too short (<15 nt): Tm too low — primers anneal non-specifically, amplifying wrong sequences.
  • Self-complementary primers: Form hairpin structures → cannot bind template → no amplification.
  • Primer-dimers: Two primers bind each other → amplify nothing useful.
  • GC >70%: Very high Tm, primers may not dissociate cleanly.

3. Taq Polymerase (Enzyme)

📖 Taq Polymerase
Taq polymerase is a thermostable DNA polymerase originally isolated from the thermophilic bacterium Thermus aquaticus, found in hot springs. It can copy DNA strands using primers and dNTPs.

Key Properties

  • Thermostable: Remains functional and stable at temperatures up to 95°C — essential for surviving the denaturation step of PCR without needing fresh enzyme each cycle.
  • High processivity: Can synthesize long stretches of DNA without falling off the template.
  • Exonuclease activity: Has 5′→3′ exonuclease activity only — it lacks 3′→5′ (proofreading) exonuclease activity. This means Taq can make errors (it is not a high-fidelity enzyme).
  • Other thermostable polymerases (Pfu, Vent) also used — Pfu has proofreading activity (lower error rate).
❗ Clinical Significance of No Proofreading
Because Taq lacks 3′→5′ exonuclease (proofreading) activity, it has an error rate of ~1 mistake per 10,000 bases. For applications requiring high accuracy (cloning, site-directed mutagenesis), high-fidelity polymerases like Pfu are preferred.

4. dNTPs (Deoxyribonucleoside Triphosphates)

  • The four building blocks of DNA: dATP, dCTP, dGTP, dTTP.
  • Taq polymerase uses these to synthesize the new complementary strand.
  • Must be present in equimolar concentrations for balanced incorporation.
  • The triphosphate group provides the energy for phosphodiester bond formation.

5. Mg²⁺ (Magnesium Ions)

  • An essential cofactor for Taq polymerase activity.
  • Mg²⁺ concentration significantly affects PCR specificity and yield.
  • Too little Mg²⁺ → enzyme inactive → no product.
  • Too much Mg²⁺ → reduced specificity → non-specific bands.
  • Typically optimized at 1.5–2.5 mM.

6. Buffers

  • Maintain optimal pH (~8.3–8.8) for Taq polymerase activity.
  • Provide stable ionic conditions throughout the cycling reaction.
  • Usually supplied by the manufacturer with the enzyme (e.g., 10× PCR buffer contains Tris-HCl, KCl).

📌 Section Summary

  • DNA template provides the sequence to be copied; up to 3 kb in standard PCR.
  • Two primers (17–30 nt, ~50% GC) define the amplification boundaries; must not be self-complementary or complementary to each other.
  • Taq polymerase is thermostable (up to 95°C), has 5′→3′ exo only, lacks proofreading.
  • dNTPs are the building blocks; Mg²⁺ is a critical cofactor; buffer maintains pH/ionic conditions.

The Three Steps of the PCR Cycle

↑ Top

All PCR components are mixed in a tube, which is placed in a thermal cycler (a programmable heating system that precisely controls temperature changes). The reaction proceeds through three repeated steps:

Step 1 — Denaturation

95°C
Heat — Strand Separation

Step 2 — Annealing

40–50°C
Cool — Primer Binding

Step 3 — Extension

72°C
Synthesis — DNA Copying

Step 1: Denaturation (95°C)

The reaction mixture is heated to approximately 90–95°C. At this temperature, the hydrogen bonds holding the two strands of the DNA double helix together are broken. The double-stranded DNA template separates into two single strands. These single strands will serve as templates for new DNA synthesis.

✅ Why 95°C?
The temperature must be high enough to fully melt (denature) all double-stranded regions, including GC-rich areas (which need more energy to separate because G-C base pairs have 3 hydrogen bonds vs 2 for A-T). 95°C reliably denatures most DNA templates. Taq polymerase survives this temperature.

Step 2: Primer Annealing (40–50°C)

The temperature is reduced to allow the primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the single-stranded template DNA. Each primer anneals to one strand at the boundary of the target region.

  • The forward primer anneals to the antisense (−) strand at the 5′ boundary of the target.
  • The reverse primer anneals to the sense (+) strand at the 3′ boundary of the target.
⚠️ Annealing Temperature Is Critical
  • Too low (e.g., 37°C): Primers anneal non-specifically (to wrong sequences) → spurious products.
  • Too high (close to Tm of primer): Primers don't bind at all → no product.
  • Optimal annealing T is typically 5°C below the melting temperature (Tm) of the primers.

Step 3: Extension / Elongation (72°C)

Taq DNA polymerase is active at 72°C (its optimal temperature). Starting from the 3′-OH of each annealed primer, Taq adds complementary dNTPs in the 5′→3′ direction, synthesizing a new DNA strand complementary to each template strand.

  • The new strand grows by adding nucleotides that are complementary to the template (A with T, G with C).
  • Each primer extension produces a copy of the target region.
  • At the end of Step 3, each original template has been copied, resulting in two double-stranded DNA molecules.
✅ Why 72°C?
72°C is the optimal temperature for Taq polymerase activity. At this temperature, the enzyme processes at maximum speed (~1,000 bp per minute) with good fidelity. The temperature is high enough that primers don't anneal non-specifically, but low enough that the newly synthesized strand remains stable.

Cycle Completion and Repetition

After Step 3, the cycle is re-started by returning to the denaturation temperature. The thermal cycler automates this cycle typically 25–35 times. Each cycle doubles the amount of target DNA — this is exponential amplification.

🧮 Automation
Automation of the PCR cycle is achieved using a thermal cycler — a programmable heating system that precisely controls and rapidly changes the reaction temperature. This replaced the original manual method of moving tubes between different water baths.
StepTemperatureWhat HappensDuration (typical)

📌 Section Summary

  • Denaturation (95°C): DNA strands separate.
  • Annealing (40–50°C): Primers bind to their complementary sequences.
  • Extension (72°C): Taq polymerase synthesizes new DNA strands from primers.
  • Each cycle doubles the target DNA — exponential amplification.
  • A thermal cycler automates the temperature changes.

Target Amplification — The Mathematics

↑ Top

Exponential Amplification

PCR amplification is exponential, not linear. After each cycle, the number of target DNA copies doubles. Starting from a single template molecule (or a small number of molecules):

🧮 Amplification Formula
Number of Copies = 2ⁿ
where n = number of cycles

✅ Practical Insight
After just 30 cycles (which takes about 1–3 hours in a modern thermal cycler), a single DNA molecule is amplified to over one billion copies. This is sufficient for gel electrophoresis, sequencing, cloning, and virtually all downstream applications.

Amplicon — What Is It?

📖 Amplicon
An amplicon is the specific DNA fragment produced (amplified) by PCR. It is defined and delimited at each end by the primer sequences. Its size is fixed by the positions of the two primers on the template, and it includes the primer sequences themselves plus the intervening sequence.

Why Not Just Keep Cycling Forever?

  • Taq polymerase gradually loses activity over many cycles (though it survives individual denaturation steps).
  • dNTPs and primers become depleted as they are incorporated.
  • Accumulation of pyrophosphate inhibits the reaction.
  • In practice, 25–35 cycles give excellent yields without diminishing returns.

📌 Section Summary

  • PCR amplification is exponential: n cycles produce 2ⁿ copies.
  • 30 cycles → ~1 billion copies from a single template molecule.
  • The amplicon is the specific PCR product defined by the two primer positions.
  • Practical PCR uses 25–35 cycles before efficiency drops.

Section 13

Detection of Products & Applications of PCR

↑ Top

How Are PCR Products Detected?

After amplification, the products must be analysed. Three main methods are used:

1
Gel Electrophoresis

The most common method. The PCR product is run on an agarose gel, stained with EtBr, and visualized under UV. A single band of the expected size confirms successful amplification. Multiple bands or smearing suggests non-specific amplification.

2
Sequencing of the Amplified Fragment

The PCR product can be directly sequenced (e.g., by Sanger sequencing) to confirm its identity and exact nucleotide composition. This is the gold standard for confirmation.

3
Southern Blot (and variants)

The PCR product can be blotted and probed for additional confirmation of sequence identity, especially when multiple products are present.

Applications of PCR

PCR has transformed virtually every area of biology and medicine. Here are the key applications:

✅ Why PCR Is Revolutionary
Before PCR (invented by Kary Mullis, 1983), studying specific DNA sequences required large amounts of DNA, restriction cloning, and weeks of work. PCR achieves the same amplification in a few hours from trace amounts of DNA — even from ancient specimens, single cells, or degraded samples.

🎓 Master Revision Summary

PART 1 — Gel Electrophoresis

  • Purpose: Separate DNA/RNA/protein by size using electric current through a gel sieve.
  • Principle: DNA = negatively charged (phosphate) → moves toward + electrode. Smaller fragments travel farther.
  • SAGE: Submerged Agarose Gel Electrophoresis — gel covered in buffer; standard technique.
  • Visualization: Ethidium bromide (intercalating dye) → orange fluorescence under UV.
  • Marker dye: Bromophenol blue tracks gel progress.
  • Sizing: Compare to DNA ladder (known size standards) run in parallel lane.

Agarose vs PAGE

  • Agarose: from seaweed, 0.3–2.0%, large pores, for large nucleic acids/proteins.
  • PAGE: acrylamide polymer, small pores, for small oligonucleotides and proteins.
  • Both are electrically neutral.
  • Higher concentration = smaller pores = separates smaller molecules.

SDS-PAGE

  • SDS denatures proteins + imparts uniform negative charge/mass ratio.
  • Mercaptoethanol breaks disulfide bonds (separates subunits).
  • Proteins separated by size only (like DNA in agarose).

Southern (DNA) & Northern (RNA) Blotting

  • Gel electrophoresis → transfer to nitrocellulose membrane → hybridize with labelled probe.
  • Probe is complementary to target sequence → detects specific band.
  • Southern = DNA; Northern = RNA.

PART 2 — PCR

  • PCR: In vitro exponential amplification of specific DNA region.
  • Components: DNA template, 2 primers, Taq polymerase, dNTPs, Mg²⁺, buffer.
  • Primers: 17–30 nt, ~50% GC, complementary to 3′ ends of target, not to each other.
  • Taq polymerase: Thermostable (≤95°C), 5′→3′ exo only, no proofreading.

The 3 PCR Steps

  • Denaturation (95°C): Double-stranded DNA → single strands.
  • Annealing (40–50°C): Primers bind to complementary template sequences.
  • Extension (72°C): Taq synthesizes new DNA strands from primers.

PCR Amplification Mathematics

Copies after n cycles = 2ⁿ   |   30 cycles → ~1,073,741,824 copies

PCR Detection & Applications

  • Detection: Gel electrophoresis, sequencing, Southern blot.
  • Applications: Genome mapping, biodiversity, diagnostics (prenatal, cancer, viral), drug resistance, forensics.
