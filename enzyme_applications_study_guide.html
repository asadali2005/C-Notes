Section 01

Plasma Enzymes Overview

Enzymes are found in essentially every tissue and fluid in the body. When cells are damaged, these intracellular enzymes leak into the bloodstream, raising their plasma concentrations. Measuring these levels provides critical diagnostic information. To understand this clinically, we first classify plasma enzymes into two major groups.

Two Major Groups of Plasma Enzymes

🏭
Group 1

Actively Secreted Enzymes

A small group actively secreted into blood by specific cell types. The liver secretes zymogens (inactive enzyme precursors) involved in blood coagulation. These have a genuine physiological role in plasma.

🔓
Group 2

Released from Cell Turnover

A large number released from cells during normal cell turnover. These enzymes function intracellularly and have no physiologic use in plasma — their presence there is incidental.

🔑 Definition — Zymogens

Zymogens (also called proenzymes) are inactive precursors of enzymes. They require a specific biochemical event (often proteolytic cleavage) to become active. The liver secretes zymogens of coagulation factors so that clotting only occurs when needed — not spontaneously in plasma. Examples include prothrombin → thrombin, pepsinogen → pepsin.

Steady State in Healthy Individuals

In a healthy person, intracellular enzymes are released in small amounts due to normal cell turnover and are simultaneously cleared from plasma at the same rate. This creates a dynamic equilibrium (steady state).

⚖️ The Steady State Principle

Rate of enzyme release from cells → Plasma is balanced by Rate of enzyme removal from plasma

∴ In healthy individuals, plasma enzyme levels remain constant and low.

When disease or trauma causes accelerated cell death (necrosis), the rate of release overwhelms the removal rate → Elevated plasma enzyme levels → Diagnostic indicator of tissue damage.

✅ Clinical Implication

The degree of elevation of a plasma enzyme often correlates with the extent of tissue damage, making it useful not only for diagnosis but also for prognosis — predicting patient outcomes.

📌 Section 1 Summary

  • Plasma enzymes fall into two groups: (1) actively secreted (e.g., coagulation zymogens from liver), and (2) released during normal cell turnover.
  • In health, enzyme release = enzyme removal → constant plasma levels (steady state).
  • Tissue damage → increased cell necrosis → elevated plasma enzyme levels → clinically diagnostic.
  • Elevation magnitude ∝ tissue damage extent → useful for prognosis.

Section 02

Enzymes in Disease Diagnosis

A. Alteration of Plasma Enzyme Levels in Disease

Many diseases cause tissue damage, leading to increased release of intracellular enzymes into plasma. Clinicians routinely measure the activities of these enzymes to diagnose diseases affecting the:

  • Heart (myocardial infarction)
  • Liver (hepatitis, cirrhosis, jaundice)
  • Skeletal muscle (muscular dystrophy, rhabdomyolysis)
  • Pancreas (acute pancreatitis)
  • Prostate (prostatic cancer)
  • Bone (rickets, osteomalacia)
⚠️ Important Concept

Enzyme activities are measured in plasma (serum) — rarely from tissue biopsies — because blood tests are non-invasive and practical. The enzyme activity level in plasma reflects the sum total of enzyme leaking from all damaged cells in the body.

B. Plasma Enzymes as Diagnostic Tools — Tissue Specificity

The key principle behind enzyme diagnostics is tissue specificity: certain enzymes are highly concentrated in one or a few tissues. Therefore, elevated plasma levels of that enzyme point directly to damage in the corresponding tissue.

🔑 Classic Example — ALT and the Liver

Alanine aminotransferase (ALT) is found in high concentrations in liver cells. When hepatic tissue is damaged (e.g., in viral hepatitis, toxic hepatitis, liver cirrhosis), ALT leaks massively into plasma. Elevated plasma ALT is therefore a sensitive and specific signal of liver damage.

ALT is preferred over AST for liver disease because ALT is more liver-specific; AST is also found in heart and muscle.

Diagnostic Enzyme Reference Table

Enzyme (Abbreviation) Primary Location in Body Cause of Elevated Plasma Level
Acid Phosphatase (ACP) Prostate Prostatic cancer
Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) Bone, Liver Rickets, hypoparathyroidism, osteomalacia, obstructive jaundice, bone/liver cancer
Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT) Liver (also muscle, heart, kidney) Hepatitis, jaundice, circulatory failure with liver congestion
Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST) Heart, muscle, red cells, liver Myocardial infarction, muscle damage, anemia, hepatitis, circulatory failure
Amylase (AM) Pancreas Acute pancreatitis, peptic ulcer
γ-Glutamyl Transferase (GGT / GMT) Liver, kidney, pancreas Hepatitis, alcoholic liver damage, cholestasis
✅ Why Both ALT and AST are Measured

AST is present in heart, muscle, liver, and red blood cells — it is not liver-specific. ALT is predominantly in the liver. The AST:ALT ratio (De Ritis ratio) helps distinguish liver disease types: a ratio > 2 suggests alcoholic liver disease; a ratio < 1 suggests viral hepatitis. This illustrates why enzyme panels (not single enzymes) are often used clinically.

📌 Section 2 Summary

  • Tissue damage → intracellular enzyme leakage into plasma → elevated enzyme levels detectable by blood tests.
  • Tissue-specific enzymes allow pinpointing the site of damage (ALT → liver; ACP → prostate; Amylase → pancreas).
  • Both the identity and the degree of elevation of the enzyme provide diagnostic and prognostic information.
  • Enzyme assays are performed on plasma/serum — not tissue — making them minimally invasive.

Section 03

Isoenzymes & Cardiac Disease

C. What Are Isoenzymes?

🔑 Definition — Isoenzymes (Isozymes)

Isoenzymes are multiple molecular forms of the same enzyme — they catalyze the identical biochemical reaction but differ in their physical and chemical properties, including:

  • Amino acid sequence (due to genetic differences)
  • Number and type of charged amino acid residues
  • Electrophoretic mobility (migration speed in an electric field)
  • Immunological properties, Km, and thermal stability

Why Do Isoenzymes Exist?

Different tissues express different gene variants (alleles) or different subunit combinations of the same enzyme gene family. This allows the same catalytic function to be "fine-tuned" for the metabolic needs and environment of each tissue. The tissue-specific pattern of isoenzymes is genetically determined and highly reproducible.

How Are They Detected?

Because isoenzymes differ in the number of charged amino acids, they migrate differently under an electric field. Electrophoresis is the gold-standard method for separating and identifying isoenzymes. A blood sample is placed on a gel, current applied, and each isoenzyme migrates to a characteristic position that can be visualized with enzyme-specific staining.

💡 Key Concept — Organ Fingerprinting

Different organs contain characteristic proportions of different isoenzymes. The pattern of isoenzymes appearing in plasma after disease therefore acts as an "organ fingerprint" — pointing to the specific tissue that was damaged. This is far more specific than measuring total enzyme activity.

Creatine Kinase (CK) Isoenzymes

Creatine kinase (CK) is the most clinically important isoenzyme system for cardiac diagnosis. CK catalyzes the reversible phosphorylation of creatine to phosphocreatine — crucial for rapid ATP regeneration in muscle.

Quaternary Structure of CK Isoenzymes

CK is a dimer — composed of two polypeptide subunits. There are two types of subunits:

  • B subunit — found predominantly in brain
  • M subunit — found predominantly in muscle

These two subunit types combine into three possible dimers, creating three CK isoenzymes:

CK1 (BB)

Fastest migration toward anode
CK2 (MB)

⚡ Tissue Distribution Summary

Brain: Virtually all CK is CK1 (BB)

Skeletal muscle: Virtually all CK is CK3 (MM)

Cardiac muscle: ~one-third is CK2 (MB), the rest is CK3 (MM)

The presence of CK2 (MB) in plasma is virtually specific for myocardial damage because no other tissue contains >5% of its CK as CK2 (MB).

Diagnosis of Myocardial Infarction (MI)

🔑 Definition — Myocardial Infarction

A myocardial infarction (MI, "heart attack") occurs when blood flow to a portion of the heart muscle is blocked, causing ischemia and necrosis of cardiac tissue. Dead heart muscle cells release their intracellular contents — including CK and troponins — into the bloodstream.

CK2 (MB) as a Diagnostic Marker

Because cardiac muscle uniquely contains significant CK2 (MB), the appearance of this isoenzyme in plasma is virtually diagnostic of myocardial infarction. It is especially useful when the ECG (electrocardiogram) is difficult to interpret — for example, when the patient has a history of previous heart disease that alters baseline ECG readings.

Time Course of CK2 (MB) After Acute MI

0 hours — Onset of Chest Pain

Myocardial infarction begins. Cardiac muscle cells start dying due to ischemia.

4–8 hours — First Appearance

CK2 (MB) begins to appear in plasma. This is the earliest window for biochemical detection.

~24 hours — Peak Activity

CK2 (MB) reaches maximum plasma concentration. Peak corresponds to maximal cell necrosis.

48–72 hours — Return to Baseline

CK2 (MB) levels normalize. If CK2 stays elevated, it may suggest ongoing or re-infarction.

Troponins — Superior Cardiac Markers

🔑 What Are Troponins?

Troponins are regulatory proteins of the contractile apparatus in cardiac and skeletal muscle. They control muscle contraction by regulating the interaction of actin and myosin. The cardiac troponin complex has three subunits:

  • Troponin T (TnT) — binds to tropomyosin
  • Troponin I (TnI) — inhibitory subunit, inhibits actomyosin ATPase
  • Troponin C (TnC) — calcium-binding subunit that triggers contraction

Cardiac Troponin I (cTnI) — The Gold Standard

Cardiac troponin I (cTnI) is the most sensitive and specific marker currently available for myocardial damage. Cardiac isoforms of troponin T and I are unique to cardiac muscle — they are not expressed in adult skeletal muscle — making them highly specific.

⚡ cTnI Time Course After MI

Appears: 4–6 hours after MI onset

Peaks: 8–28 hours after MI onset

Remains elevated: 3–10 days (much longer than CK2!)

This prolonged elevation window makes troponins especially useful for diagnosing MI in patients who present late (e.g., 48–72 hours after symptom onset), when CK2 levels would already have normalized.

CK2 (MB) vs. Troponin — Comparison

Property CK2 (MB) Cardiac Troponin I (cTnI)
First appearance after MI 4–8 hours 4–6 hours
Peak time ~24 hours 8–28 hours
Duration of elevation 48–72 hours 3–10 days ✅ (much longer)
Cardiac specificity High (>5% CK as MB = cardiac) Very high — cardiac-specific isoform

✅ Clinical Bottom Line

Elevated serum troponins are more predictive of adverse outcomes in unstable angina or myocardial infarction than the conventional CK2 assay. Modern cardiac care uses high-sensitivity troponin assays as the primary biomarker, with CK-MB as a useful secondary or confirmatory test (especially for detecting re-infarction, since troponin remains elevated too long).

Lactate Dehydrogenase (LD) Isoenzymes

Lactate dehydrogenase (LD) is another key isoenzyme system. LD catalyzes the interconversion of lactate and pyruvate. It exists as five isoenzymes (LD1–LD5), all tetramers made from two types of subunits (H and M).

Isoenzyme Primary Location Elevated In
LD1 > LD2 Heart, kidney, blood cells Myocardial infarction, kidney disease, megaloblastic anemia, leukemia
LD2, LD3 Leukocytes Leukemia
LD5 Liver, skeletal muscle Liver disease, muscle damage
💡 LD Flip Pattern in MI

Normally in plasma, LD2 > LD1. In myocardial infarction, heart-specific LD1 floods the plasma, causing the pattern to flip so that LD1 > LD2. This is historically called the "LD flip" and was used diagnostically before troponins became available.

📌 Section 3 Summary

  • Isoenzymes catalyze the same reaction but differ in physical/chemical properties due to genetic differences in amino acid sequence.
  • They can be separated by electrophoresis based on different electrophoretic mobilities.
  • CK has 3 isoenzymes: CK1 (BB, brain), CK2 (MB, cardiac), CK3 (MM, skeletal muscle).
  • CK2 (MB) >5% of total CK is virtually diagnostic of myocardial infarction.
  • CK2 appears 4–8h, peaks at 24h, returns to baseline at 48–72h after MI.
  • Cardiac troponin I (cTnI) is more sensitive and specific; appears at 4–6h, peaks at 8–28h, remains elevated for 3–10 days.
  • Elevated troponins are more predictive of adverse outcomes than CK2 in unstable angina and MI.
  • LD isoenzymes: LD1>LD2 (LD flip) is characteristic of MI.

Section 04

Enzymes as Analytical Agents

Principles: Sensitivity & Specificity

Enzymes are powerful analytical tools because of two key properties that make them superior to many chemical reagents:

🎯
Property 1

Specificity

Each enzyme binds to only one specific substrate (or a narrow range). This means an enzyme-based test will detect only the target molecule, even in a complex biological mixture containing many similar molecules.

🔍
Property 2

Sensitivity

Enzymes can detect extremely low concentrations of substrate — even trace amounts. This is critical for detecting disease markers or metabolites that may be present at very low levels in blood or urine.

🔑 Immobilised Enzymes

In analytical (and industrial) applications, enzymes are often immobilized — physically attached to a solid support (such as cellulose, beads, or a membrane). Immobilization offers two major advantages:

  • Reusability: The enzyme doesn't get washed away in the reaction — it stays in place and can be used repeatedly (economical).
  • Protection: Attachment stabilizes the enzyme, protecting it from denaturation and degradation during use.

Glucose Detection Using Clinistix

Why Not Use Benedict's Test?

Benedict's test detects all reducing sugars (glucose, fructose, galactose, lactose, maltose) — it is not specific for glucose. An enzyme-based method using glucose oxidase detects only glucose, which is essential for diagnosing and monitoring diabetes mellitus.

The Clinistix Device

The clinistix is a plastic strip used to detect glucose in urine samples of diabetic patients. At the tip is a cellulose fibre pad containing three immobilized components:

  • Glucose oxidase — converts glucose to gluconic acid + H₂O₂
  • Peroxidase — uses the H₂O₂ to oxidize the chromagen dye
  • Chromagen dye (DH₂) — a colorless dye that becomes colored when oxidized

The Two-Step Reaction

Step 1 (Glucose Oxidase): Glucose + O₂ → Gluconic acid + H₂O₂ Step 2 (Peroxidase): DH₂ (colorless chromagen dye) + H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + D (colored product) Net effect: Glucose concentration → Amount of H₂O₂ → Intensity of color change
✅ Reading the Result

The color that develops on the clinistix pad is compared against a color chart. The color ranges from light pink (no glucose) through progressively deeper purple/blue shades as glucose concentration increases. The intensity of color is directly proportional to the glucose concentration in the urine sample.

⚡ Why This Test Works

The entire method relies on the specificity of glucose oxidase — it catalyzes the oxidation of glucose and no other sugar. This ensures that only glucose, not other reducing sugars in urine, produces the color reaction. The sequential enzyme reactions amplify the signal, making even small amounts of glucose detectable.

Enzymes in Clinical Laboratory Assays

Enzymes are incorporated into commercial diagnostic kits and diagnostic strips for the quantitative measurement of many clinically important substances in blood, plasma, serum, and urine. The enzyme-based method is more specific than chemical methods because the presence of chemically related substances does not interfere.

Analyte (Substance Measured) Enzyme(s) Used Sample Type
Glucose Glucose oxidase, Peroxidase Blood, plasma, serum, urine
Cholesterol Cholesterol esterase, Cholesterol oxidase, Peroxidase Blood, plasma, serum
Urea Urease Blood, plasma, serum
Proof of glucose in urine Glucose oxidase (strips) Blood or urine (diagnostic strips)
Immunochemical markers (ELISA) Peroxidase, Alkaline phosphatase Serum, tissue fluid

📌 Section 4 Summary

  • Enzymes are ideal analytical agents due to their high specificity (detect one substrate) and high sensitivity (detect trace amounts).
  • Immobilization on solid supports allows economical, reusable enzyme-based tests.
  • Clinistix uses immobilized glucose oxidase + peroxidase + chromagen dye to specifically detect glucose in urine — unlike non-specific Benedict's test.
  • Color intensity on clinistix ∝ glucose concentration; read against a color chart.
  • Enzyme-based kits measure glucose, cholesterol, urea, and many other analytes in clinical labs — more specific than chemical methods.

Section 05

Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA)

Principle of ELISA

🔑 Definition — ELISA

ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay) is a highly sensitive and specific technique used to detect and quantify a specific molecule (antigen or antibody) in a biological sample. It combines the specificity of antibodies with the sensitivity of enzyme-based detection. The enzyme serves as a marker (reporter) — when it catalyzes a colorless substrate into a colored product, the intensity of color indicates the amount of target molecule present.

ELISA is used in a vast array of clinical and research applications including: HIV testing, pregnancy tests (hCG detection), food allergen testing, drug detection, hormone quantification, and detection of infectious agents.

Generalized Indirect ELISA Protocol — Step by Step

The indirect ELISA detects antibodies or antigens using two antibodies. Here is a detailed breakdown of each step:

1

Bind Sample

Bind the sample (containing the target antigen) to a solid support — usually a plastic microtiter plate (96 wells). Wash to remove unbound molecules.

2

Add Primary Antibody

Add the primary antibody (specific to the target antigen). It binds to the antigen. Wash to remove unbound primary antibody.

3

Add Secondary Antibody-Enzyme

Add the secondary antibody conjugated to an enzyme. The secondary antibody binds to the primary antibody. Wash to remove unbound conjugate.

4

Add Substrate

Add the colorless substrate. The enzyme converts it to a colored product.

5

Measure Color

Observe or measure the colored product (spectrophotometrically). More antigen = more antibody binding = more enzyme = more color.

Antibodies Used in ELISA

Antibody Source Target Role
Primary Antibody (1°) Rabbit immunized with target antigen The specific target antigen Captures the target molecule; provides specificity
Secondary Antibody (2°) Goat immunized with rabbit antibodies; conjugated to enzyme The primary antibody (rabbit IgG) Amplifies signal; carries the enzyme reporter

Enzymes Used in ELISA

The enzyme conjugated to the secondary antibody converts a colorless substrate to a colored product. Commonly used enzymes include:

  • Alkaline phosphatase — converts p-nitrophenyl phosphate (colorless) to p-nitrophenol (yellow)
  • Horseradish peroxidase (HRP) — converts TMB or ABTS substrates to blue/green colored products
  • Urease — produces ammonia which can be detected colorimetrically
💡 Why Two Antibodies?

The secondary antibody serves as a signal amplifier. Multiple secondary antibody molecules (each carrying an enzyme) can bind to each primary antibody molecule, greatly amplifying the signal. This is why ELISA can detect tiny amounts of antigen — even picogram (10⁻¹²g) quantities. The secondary antibody also makes the system modular — the same enzyme-conjugated secondary antibody can be reused across many different primary antibodies targeting different antigens.

⚠️ Key Points to Remember

• The primary antibody binds the antigen (target-specific step).

• The secondary antibody binds the primary antibody (not the antigen directly).

• The enzyme is on the secondary antibody — not the primary antibody.

• Each wash step is critical — unbound antibody must be removed to prevent false-positive results.

• Color intensity is directly proportional to the amount of target antigen present.

📌 Section 5 Summary

  • ELISA detects/quantifies specific molecules using the combined specificity of antibodies and sensitivity of enzyme catalysis.
  • Five steps: bind antigen → primary antibody → secondary antibody-enzyme conjugate → colorless substrate → colored product → measure.
  • Primary antibody (from rabbit) binds target antigen; secondary antibody (from goat, enzyme-conjugated) binds primary antibody.
  • Common enzymes: alkaline phosphatase, peroxidase, urease — all convert colorless substrates to colored products.
  • The two-antibody system amplifies signal enormously, enabling detection of trace amounts.
  • 96-well microtiter plates allow high-throughput testing of many samples simultaneously.

Section 06

Enzymotherapy

🔑 Definition — Enzymotherapy

Enzymotherapy is the use of enzymes as therapeutic agents (drugs). It represents a novel approach where enzymes — usually of plant and/or animal origin — are administered to patients to treat a range of disorders. This is distinct from diagnostics; here, the enzyme actively participates in the treatment.

Conditions Treated by Orally Administered Enzymes

🫁

Digestive & GI Disorders

Enzyme preparations help digest food when the patient's own digestive enzymes are insufficient (e.g., pancreatic insufficiency, cystic fibrosis).

🔥

Inflammatory Diseases & Edema

Proteolytic enzymes have anti-inflammatory properties and can reduce edema (swelling) by breaking down inflammatory mediators and fibrin deposits.

🛡️

Immune & Autoimmune Diseases

Certain enzyme mixtures modulate immune responses. Used in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, and inflammatory bowel disease.

🦠

Viral Diseases

Enzyme therapy has been explored for viral conditions including herpes and HIV/AIDS, where enzymes may degrade viral coat proteins or modify immune responses.

🎗️

Cancer

Proteolytic enzymes have shown activity against certain tumors, potentially by degrading the extracellular matrix that protects tumors or by modulating immune surveillance.

How Enzymotherapy is Administered

✅ Formulation Details

Oral enzymotherapy uses mixtures of enzymes of plant and/or animal origin — typically including proteinases (proteases), amylase, and lipase. These are administered as acid-resistant (enteric-coated) tablets.

Why acid-resistant? Without enteric coating, the enzyme proteins would be denatured and inactivated by the strongly acidic environment of the stomach (pH 1–2). The acid-resistant coating protects the enzyme until it reaches the small intestine (pH 6–7), where it dissolves and the enzyme becomes active.

⚡ Specific Example: Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement

Patients with chronic pancreatitis or cystic fibrosis cannot produce sufficient pancreatic enzymes (lipase, amylase, proteases). They develop fat malabsorption (steatorrhea) and malnutrition. Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) provides these enzymes in enteric-coated capsules, restoring digestive function.

📌 Section 6 Summary

  • Enzymotherapy = use of enzymes as therapeutic drugs (new approach to treatment).
  • Orally administered enzymes treat digestive, GI, inflammatory, immune/autoimmune, viral diseases, and cancer.
  • Formulated as mixtures of proteinases, amylase, and lipase from plant and/or animal sources.
  • Administered as acid-resistant (enteric-coated) tablets to prevent gastric acid denaturation.

Section 07

Industrial & Biotechnology Applications

Enzymes in Biotechnology

Biotechnology harnesses enzymes to perform chemical transformations that would otherwise require harsh, expensive, or polluting chemistry. Two essential properties for industrial enzymes are:

🌡️

Thermostability

Enzymes used in industry must withstand elevated temperatures (often used in high-throughput, heated processes). Thermostable enzymes from thermophilic bacteria are often selected or engineered for industrial use.

⚗️

pH Stability

Industrial processes may occur at extremes of pH. Enzymes must be stable and active across the relevant pH range of their application (e.g., alkaline proteases in detergents).

♻️

Immobilization

Immobilizing enzymes on solid supports allows economical operation — the enzyme can be reused many times rather than being lost with each batch reaction. It also protects the enzyme from damage.

Industrial Goal: Novel Enzymatic Activity

The goal of industrial enzyme biotechnology is to create an organism with novel enzymatic activity that can convert an existing substrate into a commercial compound by combining:

  • Chemical treatments
  • Fermentation steps using microorganisms

Synthesis of L-Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C)

🔑 Background — L-Ascorbic Acid

L-Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) is an essential micronutrient for humans — required for collagen synthesis, immune function, and as an antioxidant. Humans cannot synthesize it endogenously and must obtain it from diet or supplementation. Industrial production is therefore of enormous commercial importance.

The Traditional Commercial Synthesis Process

Vitamin C is synthesized from D-glucose using a combination of chemical and microbial steps. The traditional route includes one microbial fermentation step and a number of chemical steps, making it an expensive process.

Key Steps in the Synthesis Pathway

D-Glucose → D-Sorbitol via catalytic reduction (using H₂). This is a purely chemical step.
D-Sorbitol → L-Sorbose via microbial fermentation using Acetobacter suboxydans, which produces the enzyme sorbitol dehydrogenase (using NAD/NADH₂). This is the key biological step.
L-Sorbose → 2-KLG (2-keto-L-gluconic acid) via chemical oxidation using O₂, followed by additional chemical treatment (acid treatment) to convert intermediates.
2-KLG → L-Ascorbic Acid via acid treatment to convert the sodium salt (enol form) to the final product.

The Genetically Engineered (Biotechnological) Shortcut

A more efficient approach uses two microorganisms to produce the key intermediate 2-KLG directly from D-glucose:

⚡ Two-Organism Strategy

Erwinia contains a set of three enzymes that convert D-glucose → 2,5-DKG (2,5-diketo-gluconic acid).

Corynebacterium contains an enzyme (2,5-DKG reductase) that converts 2,5-DKG → 2-KLG.

Therefore, 2-KLG (the immediate precursor of L-ascorbic acid) can be produced from D-glucose by:

  • Co-cultivating both microorganisms together, OR
  • Genetically engineering Erwinia to express the Corynebacterium enzyme — combining both steps in a single organism, making the process much more efficient.

Improving 2,5-DKG Reductase

The commercial utility of the cloned 2,5-DKG reductase gene can be further improved by protein engineering — replacing certain amino acids to create mutant enzymes with:

  • Increased catalytic activity (higher reaction rate)
  • Enhanced thermal stability (more durable in industrial conditions)

Microbial Synthesis of Indigo

🔑 Background — Indigo

Indigo is a deep blue dye historically extracted from plants (Indigofera species). It is now famous as the dye in blue jeans. Traditional chemical synthesis of indigo uses toxic chemicals (e.g., aniline) and generates significant industrial waste. Microbial biosynthesis offers a cleaner, enzymatic alternative.

Microbial Background

Certain bacteria — most notably Pseudomonas species — can use organic compounds like naphthalene, toluene, xylene, and phenol as their sole carbon source. The genes encoding the enzymes for degrading these compounds are often located on large naturally occurring plasmids (approximately 50–200 kb).

Indigo Biosynthesis Pathway in Genetically Engineered E. coli

The indigo biosynthesis occurs via the following enzymatic cascade:

Tryptophan → Indole
Catalyzed by tryptophanase — an E. coli host enzyme. Tryptophan (amino acid in the growth medium) is converted to indole by elimination of pyruvate and ammonia.
Indole → cis-Indole-2,3-dihydrodiol
Catalyzed by naphthalene dioxygenase (derived from the NAH plasmid, Pathway A) or by xylene oxidase (from the TOL plasmid, Pathway B). Oxygen is incorporated into the indole ring.
cis-Indole-2,3-dihydrodiol → Indoxyl
Spontaneous dehydration — loss of water occurs without enzymatic catalysis.
Indoxyl → Indigo
Air oxidation (O₂) — spontaneous dimerization and oxidation of two indoxyl molecules to form indigo.
✅ Important Note

E. coli transformants that synthesize indigo contain either Pathway A (naphthalene dioxygenase) OR Pathway B (xylene oxidase) — but not both. The two pathways are alternative routes to the same intermediate (cis-indole-2,3-dihydrodiol).

Lactase & Lactose Intolerance

🔑 Lactase (β-Galactosidase)

Lactase is the enzyme that hydrolyzes lactose (the sugar in milk) into its component monosaccharides: glucose + galactose. Without sufficient lactase, lactose cannot be digested in the small intestine and passes to the colon where bacteria ferment it — causing symptoms of lactose intolerance.

The Lactase Reaction

Lactase (β-galactosidase): Lactose + H₂O → Glucose + Galactose This is a hydrolysis reaction.

Source of Industrial Lactase

Lactase enzyme (β-galactosidase) is extracted from fungi such as Aspergillus oryzae. It is used to produce lactose-free dairy products — milk, cheese, and yogurt — for people with lactose intolerance.

Lactose Intolerance — Explained

Aspect Detail
Cause Insufficient production of lactase enzyme in the small intestinal brush border
What happens to lactose Undigested lactose reaches the colon and is fermented by colonic bacteria, producing gas (H₂, CO₂, methane) and short-chain fatty acids
Symptoms Nausea, cramps, bloating, gas, and diarrhea — typically within 4 hours of consuming dairy products
Treatment Option 1 Take a lactase enzyme tablet with meals — the exogenous lactase digests dietary lactose in the gut
Treatment Option 2 Consume lactose-free dairy products (dairy processed with lactase enzyme prior to consumption)
Dietary management A special lactose-free diet may be necessary for severely intolerant individuals

Pectinase in the Drinks Industry

🔑 What is Pectin?

Pectin is a complex polysaccharide found in the cell walls of plants. It is abundant in fruits. When fruit is crushed to extract juice, pectin is released along with the juice, causing the juice to appear cloudy and viscous — it makes the juice thicker and harder to filter or pour.

Pectinase — Solving the Problem

Pectinase is an enzyme that breaks down pectin into smaller, soluble fragments. In the fruit juice industry, adding pectinase:

  • Clarifies the fruit juice (removes cloudiness)
  • Reduces viscosity (makes the juice flow more freely)
  • Increases the yield of juice extracted from fruit pulp

Source of Commercial Pectinase

Pectinase is obtained from the fungus Aspergillus niger, which produces pectinase as an extracellular enzyme (secreted outside the cell into the culture medium), making extraction relatively easy.

✅ Why Extracellular Production is Advantageous

Extracellular enzymes are secreted into the fermentation medium by the microorganism. This makes industrial extraction much simpler — you simply filter out the cells and collect the enzyme from the liquid. Intracellular enzymes require cell disruption and are harder and more expensive to extract.

Products of Enzyme Technology — Historical Context

Microorganisms have been used for thousands of years to produce fermented foods and beverages — without conscious knowledge of enzymes. Modern science reveals that these processes are fundamentally enzyme-driven. Products include:

🍷

Wine & Beer

Yeast enzymes (amylases, glucosidases, invertases) convert sugars to alcohol (ethanol) via fermentation.

🍞

Bread

Amylases break starch into fermentable sugars; yeast uses these sugars to produce CO₂ that leavens the dough. Proteases modify gluten structure.

🧀

Cheese

Rennet (chymosin) — a protease from calf stomach — coagulates milk proteins. Now produced by recombinant DNA technology.

🍶

Vinegar & Soy Sauce

Acetobacter enzymes oxidize ethanol to acetic acid (vinegar); Aspergillus protease/amylase enzymes are key in soy sauce production.

⚡ Shift to Pure Enzyme Sources

Many modern industrial processes have moved away from using whole microorganisms and instead use purified (isolated) enzymes extracted from microorganisms. This provides greater control over the reaction, avoids unwanted by-products, and allows immobilization for reuse. The enzyme is isolated from the microorganism and used directly as a catalyst.

📌 Section 7 Summary

  • Enzymes used in industry require thermostability and pH tolerance; immobilization improves economy and enzyme protection.
  • L-Ascorbic acid is synthesized from D-glucose using both chemical and microbial steps; the key biological step uses sorbitol dehydrogenase from Acetobacter suboxydans.
  • Genetically engineered Erwinia (with Corynebacterium enzyme) produces 2-KLG from glucose in a single organism — a major improvement.
  • Indigo is biosynthesized in engineered E. coli: tryptophan → (tryptophanase) → indole → (naphthalene dioxygenase OR xylene oxidase) → cis-indole-2,3-dihydrodiol → (spontaneous dehydration) → indoxyl → (O₂) → indigo.
  • Lactase (from Aspergillus oryzae) hydrolyzes lactose → glucose + galactose; used to produce lactose-free products and administered as tablets to lactose-intolerant patients.
  • Pectinase (from Aspergillus niger, extracellular) breaks down pectin in fruit juices → clarification and improved flow.
  • Traditional fermented products (wine, beer, cheese, bread, vinegar, soy sauce) are enzyme-driven; modern industry uses purified, isolated enzymes.

🗂️ All Key Concepts at a Glance

🩸 Plasma Enzymes

  • Two groups: (1) actively secreted (zymogens from liver for coagulation); (2) released from cell turnover (intracellular, no plasma function)
  • Healthy state = steady state (release = removal → constant low plasma levels)
  • Disease/trauma → necrosis → elevated plasma levels → diagnostic signal; magnitude ∝ extent of damage → prognostic value

🏥 Diagnostic Enzyme Table (Quick Reference)

  • ACP (Prostate) → Prostatic cancer
  • ALP (Bone, Liver) → Rickets, osteomalacia, obstructive jaundice, bone/liver cancer
  • ALT (Liver) → Hepatitis, jaundice, liver congestion
  • AST (Heart, muscle, RBCs, liver) → MI, muscle damage, anemia, hepatitis
  • Amylase (Pancreas) → Acute pancreatitis, peptic ulcer
  • GGT/GMT (Liver, kidney, pancreas) → Hepatitis, alcoholic liver damage, cholestasis

🫀 Isoenzymes

  • Same reaction, different physical/chemical properties; separated by electrophoresis
  • CK isoenzymes: CK1=BB (brain), CK2=MB (heart, ~1/3 of cardiac CK), CK3=MM (skeletal muscle)
  • CK2 (MB) appears 4–8h, peaks 24h, baseline 48–72h after MI
  • cTnI: appears 4–6h, peaks 8–28h, elevated 3–10 days → superior marker for MI
  • LD isoenzymes: LD1>LD2 (LD flip) = MI; LD5 elevated = liver/muscle disease
  • CK-MB: CK-MB cardiac isoenzyme diagnostic table — CK-MM → muscular dystrophy; LD1>LD2 → MI, kidney, megaloblastic anemia, leukemia; LD2,3 → leukemia; LD5 → liver disease, muscle damage

🧪 Analytical Agent Properties

  • Specificity: one substrate → identifies specific substance in complex samples
  • Sensitivity: detects trace concentrations
  • Immobilization: economical (reusable) + protective

🩺 Clinistix (Glucose Detection)

  • More specific than Benedict's test (which detects all reducing sugars)
  • Step 1: Glucose + O₂ → Gluconic acid + H₂O₂ (glucose oxidase)
  • Step 2: DH₂ + H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + D [colored] (peroxidase)
  • Color intensity ∝ glucose concentration; compare to color chart

💉 ELISA

  • 5 steps: bind antigen → 1° antibody → 2° antibody-enzyme → substrate → colored product → measure
  • 1° antibody from rabbit immunized with antigen; 2° antibody from goat immunized with rabbit IgG, conjugated to enzyme
  • Enzymes: alkaline phosphatase, peroxidase, urease → colorless → colored
  • 96-well microtiter plate; high sensitivity due to signal amplification by two-antibody system

💊 Enzymotherapy

  • Enzymes as drugs — for digestive/GI, inflammatory, immune/autoimmune, viral diseases, cancer
  • Mixtures of proteinases + amylase + lipase (plant/animal origin)
  • Administered as acid-resistant (enteric-coated) tablets to protect from gastric acid

🏭 Industrial Biotechnology

  • Industrial enzymes need thermostability + pH stability; immobilization improves reusability
  • Vitamin C synthesis: D-glucose → D-sorbitol (chemical) → L-sorbose (Acetobacter suboxydans / sorbitol dehydrogenase) → L-sorbose oxidation → 2-KLG → L-ascorbic acid
  • Genetically engineered Erwinia (with Corynebacterium 2,5-DKG reductase) produces 2-KLG from glucose directly
  • Indigo in E. coli: tryptophan → indole (tryptophanase) → cis-indole-2,3-dihydrodiol (naphthalene dioxygenase / xylene oxidase) → indoxyl (spontaneous dehydration) → indigo (O₂)
  • Lactase (Aspergillus oryzae): lactose → glucose + galactose; treats lactose intolerance; symptoms within 4h of dairy intake
  • Pectinase (Aspergillus niger, extracellular): breaks down pectin → clarifies fruit juice, reduces viscosity, increases yield
  • Historical fermented products (wine, beer, cheese, bread, vinegar, soy sauce) are enzyme-driven; modern industry uses purified isolated enzymes
