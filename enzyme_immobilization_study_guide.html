Section 01

What is Immobilization?

📌 Core Definition

Immobilization is the confinement or "imprisonment" of an enzyme or a whole cell within or on a distinct support/matrix, while still maintaining its catalytic activity. The support allows the surrounding medium (containing substrate, effector, or inhibitor molecules) to exchange freely with the immobilized biocatalyst.

Breaking Down the Definition

  • The enzyme/cell is physically restricted — it cannot move freely into the reaction medium.
  • Despite restriction, it remains catalytically active — it can still bind substrate and catalyze reactions.
  • The support/matrix is permeable — substrate can enter and product can exit.
  • The medium around it may contain substrates, effectors (activators or inhibitors) — all these can interact with the enzyme through the support.

Historical Milestone — First Immobilization Technology

🏛️ Historical First

The first industrial application of enzyme immobilization was the use of amino acylases from Aspergillus oryzae for the production of L-amino acids in Japan. This landmark application proved that immobilized enzymes could be industrially viable.

Two Core Advantages — Why Immobilize at All?

While there are many specific advantages (discussed in Section 2), two fundamental reasons drive the development of immobilization technology:

① Increased Functional Efficiency
  • Enzyme can be reused repeatedly
  • Continuous operation becomes possible
  • Higher substrate/enzyme ratios achievable
② Enhanced Reproducibility
  • Reaction conditions are more controlled
  • Product quality is consistent batch-to-batch
  • Process automation is easier
📋 Section Summary

Immobilization = confining enzyme/cell in a support, allowing the medium to exchange. The support holds the enzyme, the medium carries the substrates. The first industrial use was amino acylase in Japan. The two core benefits are improved efficiency and reproducibility.

💡 Why Stability Increases Upon Immobilization

When an enzyme is fixed to a support, its three-dimensional structure is physically restrained. This means it is harder for heat or pH changes to unfold the protein. The multiple attachment points (especially in covalent bonding) act like internal "stitches" that hold the enzyme in its active conformation. This is sometimes called conformational stabilization.

Section 04

Support / Matrix Materials Used in Immobilization

The support (also called matrix or carrier) is the solid material to which the enzyme or cell is attached. Choosing the right support is critical for the success of any immobilization strategy.

General Requirements of a Good Support

  • Must be able to hold the enzyme firmly (physically or chemically)
  • Should be cheap and easily available
  • Its reaction with the medium and enzyme should be minimal as possible — it should be chemically inert to avoid denaturing the enzyme or reacting with substrates/products
  • Must allow free exchange of the medium (substrate must be able to reach the enzyme)
  • Should ideally have a large surface area
  • Should be mechanically stable and resistant to microbial degradation

Three Major Categories of Support

① Natural Polymers

Derived from biological sources. Generally biocompatible, biodegradable, and have functional groups that can bind enzymes.

② Synthetic Polymers

Man-made polymers with controlled properties. They function as ion exchange resins with porous surfaces that trap and hold enzymes/cells.

  • They are insoluble in the reaction medium — they will not dissolve and contaminate the product
  • They have a porous surface which physically traps and holds the enzymes/cells
  • Porosity and surface chemistry can be precisely engineered
⚗️ Examples of Synthetic Polymer Supports
  • DEAE Cellulose (Diethylaminoethyl cellulose) — a modified cellulose with ionizable groups; widely used anion exchanger
  • Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) — inert, porous polymer; used as film or membrane support
  • UV-activated Polyethylene glycol (PEG) — cross-links under UV light to form a hydrogel for enzyme entrapment

③ Inorganic Materials

Minerals and non-biological solids. Generally very stable to heat and chemicals — useful in harsh industrial conditions.

Section 05

Types / Methods of Immobilization

There are five fundamental methods of immobilizing enzymes and cells. Each has a different mechanism of attachment, uses different materials, and has unique strengths and weaknesses.

📌 The Five Methods
  1. Adsorption — Enzyme adheres to support surface via weak bonds
  2. Covalent Bonding — Enzyme forms covalent bonds with support
  3. Entrapment — Enzyme is physically trapped within a matrix
  4. Cross-Linking (Copolymerization) — Enzymes cross-linked to each other via polyfunctional reagents
  5. Encapsulation — Enzyme enclosed within a semi-permeable membrane capsule
1

Adsorption Oldest Method

Core Concept

Adsorption is the oldest and simplest method of enzyme immobilization. Enzymes are adsorbed (attached via weak physical bonds) to the external surface of a support/carrier material. No chemical modification of the enzyme occurs.

🏛️ Historical Note

Nelson & Griffin demonstrated this technique by using charcoal to adsorb invertase (sucrase) — the enzyme that cleaves sucrose into glucose and fructose. This was the first recorded physical immobilization of an enzyme.

Bonds Involved in Adsorption

The bonds are all low-energy, non-covalent (weak) bonds:

  • Ionic interaction — electrostatic attraction between charged groups on the enzyme and charged groups on the support
  • Hydrogen bonds — between polar groups on the enzyme and the support surface
  • Van der Waals forces — very weak, short-range attractive forces between all molecules
💡 Why Weak Bonds?

Because the bonds are weak, adsorption is gentle on the enzyme — it is less disruptive to protein conformation than chemical methods. However, the same weakness means the enzyme can easily detach (desorb) from the carrier — the major disadvantage.

Types of Support / Carriers Used in Adsorption

  1. Mineral support: aluminum oxide, clay (kaolinite, bentonite)
  2. Organic support: starch
  3. Modified sepharose and ion exchange resins: DEAE-sephadex, CM-cellulose

Carrier Particle Size Matters

  • Carrier particle size must be small to provide a large surface area for enzyme binding (appreciable surface bonding)
  • Particle size range used: 500 Å to 1 mm diameter
  • Since enzymes are on the external surface, there are no pore diffusion limitations — substrate can easily access the enzyme

Four Sub-Methods of Adsorption

Sub-methodProcedure
1. Static Process Enzyme solution is allowed to contact the carrier without stirring. The enzyme simply diffuses to the carrier surface. Simple but slower and potentially less uniform.
2. Dynamic Batch Process Carrier is placed in the enzyme solution and the mixture is stirred or agitated. Better contact, faster adsorption, more uniform coating.
3. Reactor Loading Process Carrier is placed in the reactor first; then the enzyme solution is transferred to the reactor. Suitable for large-scale industrial applications.
4. Electrode Position (Electrophoretic) Process Carrier is placed near an electrode in an enzyme bath. When current is applied, the enzyme (being charged) migrates toward the electrode and is deposited on the carrier surface. Useful for charged proteins.
✅ Advantages
  • Easy to carry out — simple procedure
  • No reagents required — no chemical modification
  • Minimum activation steps
  • Comparatively cheap method
  • Less disruptive to protein than chemical methods
  • No pore diffusion limitations (enzyme on surface)
❌ Disadvantages
  • Desorption — enzyme can detach from carrier (major problem)
  • Efficiency is less than covalent methods
  • Sensitive to changes in pH, ionic strength, temperature (can disrupt weak bonds)
  • Less stable binding overall
2

Covalent Bonding Widely Used

Core Concept

Covalent bonding involves the formation of strong covalent bonds between chemical groups on the enzyme and reactive groups on the support. It is the most widely used method of enzyme immobilization because of its strong, stable attachment.

🔑 Critical Principle

The covalent bond must form through amino acid side chains that are NOT part of the active site. If the active site is modified, the enzyme loses its catalytic activity. This is the fundamental challenge of covalent immobilization — achieving attachment without touching the active site.

Chemical Groups on Enzyme That Form Covalent Bonds

These are the reactive groups on the enzyme's surface (not at the active site) that can participate in covalent bond formation with the support:

Functional GroupSource Amino Acid
Alpha-carboxyl group (–COOH)C-terminal of the enzyme
Alpha-amino group (–NH₂)N-terminal of the enzyme
Epsilon-amino groups (–NH₂)Lysine and Arginine residues
Beta & gamma-carboxyl groups (–COOH)Aspartate and Glutamate residues
Phenol ringTyrosine residues
Thiol group (–SH)Cysteine residues
Hydroxyl groups (–OH)Serine and Threonine residues
Imidazole groupHistidine residues
Indole ringTryptophan residues
💡 Most Reactive Groups

Hydroxyl (–OH) and Amino (–NH₂) groups form covalent bonds most easily with supports. This is why carriers that present these groups (like carbohydrates, amino group bearing carriers) are so popular in covalent immobilization.

Carriers Used for Covalent Bonding

  • Carbohydrates: Cellulose, DEAE-cellulose, Agarose — have free –OH groups
  • Synthetic agents: Polyacrylamide — reactive vinyl groups can be modified
  • Protein carriers: Use amino acid side chains for cross reactions
  • Amino group bearing carriers: Amino benzyl cellulose — presented –NH₂ groups react with enzyme
  • Inorganic carriers: Porous glass, silica — can be chemically silanized to add reactive groups
  • CNBr-agarose & CNBr-Sepharose: Cyanogen bromide activates agarose/sepharose to react with –NH₂ groups of the enzyme (most classic lab technique)

Three Methods of Covalent Bond Formation

MethodMechanismExample Reagent
1. Diazoation Bonding between an amino group of the support and a tyrosyl or histidyl group of the enzyme via a diazonium salt linkage Diazonium salt
2. Peptide Bond Formation A peptide bond is formed between amino/carboxyl groups of the support and the enzyme; uses peptide coupling chemistry Carbodiimides (EDC/NHS)
3. Polyfunctional Reagents A bifunctional or multifunctional reagent acts as a bridge between the amino group of the support and the amino group of the enzyme Glutaraldehyde (most common)
⚗️ Glutaraldehyde — The Most Important Cross-Linking Reagent

Glutaraldehyde (OHC–(CH₂)₃–CHO) is a 5-carbon dialdehyde with two aldehyde groups (–CHO) at each end. It is bifunctional — each aldehyde reacts with a free amino group (–NH₂). One end reacts with the amino group of the support; the other end reacts with the amino group of the enzyme. This creates a stable Schiff base linkage (–N=CH–). Glutaraldehyde is cheap, reactive, and widely available, making it the reagent of choice for both covalent immobilization and cross-linking.

✅ Advantages
  • Strong linkage — enzyme firmly held to support
  • No leakage or desorption problem
  • Comparatively simple once support is activated
  • Wide variety of supports with different functional groups available
  • Wide applicability across many enzyme types
❌ Disadvantages
  • Chemical modification of enzyme can cause functional conformation loss
  • If covalent bond forms at/near the active site → enzyme inactivation
  • Enzyme inactivation by conformational changes when reactions occur at active sites
  • Can be overcome by immobilizing in the presence of substrate or a competitive inhibitor (to protect the active site)
3

Entrapment

Core Concept

In entrapment, enzymes are physically trapped inside a matrix (a gel or polymer network). The enzyme is not chemically attached to the matrix — it is simply surrounded and confined. The matrix has pores through which substrate enters and product exits, but the pores are small enough to prevent the enzyme from escaping.

  • Bonds involved may be covalent or non-covalent (the polymer network itself may be cross-linked)
  • Matrix used will typically be a water-soluble polymer that is subsequently cross-linked to form an insoluble gel
  • The pore size of the matrix is adjusted to prevent loss of enzyme while allowing substrate/product exchange

Examples of Entrapment Matrices

Natural Gels
  • Agar
  • Gelatin
  • Carrageenan
  • Alginate (most used)
Synthetic Gels
  • Polyacrylamide gels
  • Cellulose triacetate
  • UV-PEG hydrogels
Special Notes
  • Agar & carrageenan: large pores
  • Pore size ∝ polymer conc.
  • Low MW enzymes may leak

Sub-Methods of Entrapment

  1. Inclusion in gels: Enzyme is mixed with gel-forming polymer; gel polymerizes around the enzyme. Example: acrylamide monomer + enzyme → polyacrylamide gel traps enzyme.
  2. Inclusion in fibers: Enzyme is incorporated into synthetic fiber material (e.g., cellulose acetate fibers); enzyme supported on fiber format.
  3. Inclusion in microcapsules: Enzyme entrapped in tiny spherical capsules formed by monomer mixtures such as polyamine or calcium alginate beads.
💡 Important Nuances
  • There is a possibility of leakage of low molecular weight enzymes — small enzymes can pass through the pores of the matrix
  • Agar and carrageenan have large pore sizes — better for large enzymes; risk of leakage with small ones
  • Pore size can be controlled by adjusting the concentration of polymer (higher concentration = tighter network = smaller pores)
  • Entrapment can be used for sensing applications (biosensors — enzyme trapped near electrode detects substrate)
  • Not much success in industrial processes (mainly due to leakage, diffusion limitations)
  • But easy to practice at small scale — commonly done in laboratory settings
✅ Advantages
  • Fast procedure
  • Cheap — low-cost matrix materials available (alginate, agar)
  • Mild conditions required — no harsh chemistry
  • Less chance of conformational changes in the enzyme (no chemical modification)
❌ Disadvantages
  • Leakage of enzyme — especially small MW enzymes
  • Pore diffusion limitation — substrate may not easily reach all trapped enzymes
  • Chance of microbial contamination (gel matrices can support microbial growth)
  • Not ideal for large-scale industrial processes
4

Cross-Linking (Copolymerization)

Core Concept

Cross-linking involves creating covalent bonds directly between different enzyme molecules via polyfunctional reagents. Unlike covalent bonding to a support, in cross-linking no matrix or support is involved — the enzymes form a large, insoluble aggregate by linking to each other.

📌 Definition

Cross-linking = covalent bonding between various groups of enzyme molecules via polyfunctional reagents. Enzymes cross-link to each other rather than to a carrier, forming a self-contained, insoluble biocatalyst aggregate.

How Cross-Linking Works

Enzyme A (–NH₂)
+
Glutaraldehyde (CHO–CH₂–CH₂–CH₂–CHO)
+
Enzyme B (–NH₂)
↓
Enzyme A–N=CH–(CH₂)₃–CH=N–Enzyme B

Schiff base linkage forms at both ends of glutaraldehyde → enzyme molecules are permanently cross-linked to each other

Key Features of Cross-Linking

  • Most commonly used polyfunctional reagents: Glutaraldehyde and Diazonium salt
  • Technique is cheap and simple — only a reagent is needed, no support material
  • Not often used with pure proteins — because polyfunctional reagents can react indiscriminately with any amino group, potentially deactivating enzymes
  • It is widely used in commercial preparations — especially when purity is not critical (e.g., crude enzyme preparations)

Cross-Linked Enzyme Aggregates (CLEAs) — Modern Application

🔬 Modern Development

A modern variant called Cross-Linked Enzyme Aggregates (CLEAs) involves first precipitating enzyme molecules (forming aggregates), then cross-linking the aggregate with glutaraldehyde. CLEAs are highly stable, have high enzyme loading, and no carrier dilution of activity.

✅ Advantages
  • No support/carrier needed — simpler
  • Cheap and straightforward
  • High enzyme loading per unit volume
  • Widely applicable commercially
❌ Disadvantages (Demerits)
  • Polyfunctional reagents can denature the enzyme — the reagent can react with active site residues
  • Not suitable for pure protein preparations (too harsh)
  • Difficult to control degree of cross-linking
5

Encapsulation

Core Concept

Encapsulation involves enclosing enzymes within a semi-permeable membrane capsule. The enzyme is entirely surrounded by the capsule wall. The capsule is made of a membrane that allows small substrate molecules to enter and small product molecules to exit, while retaining the large enzyme molecules inside.

📌 Key Structural Features
  • Capsule material: nitrocellulose or nylon — both form semi-permeable membranes
  • Effectiveness depends on the stability of the enclosed enzymes
  • The membrane is semi-permeable — selectively permeable to molecules below a certain size cutoff

Distinction Between Entrapment and Encapsulation

FeatureEntrapmentEncapsulation
StructurePolymer matrix (gel/fiber) throughoutDiscrete hollow capsule with membrane wall
Membrane typePorous gel matrixSemi-permeable membrane (nitrocellulose/nylon)
ScaleSmall to mediumMicro-scale (microcapsules)
Substrate accessThrough gel poresThrough membrane pores (only small molecules)
Key limitationEnzyme leakage, microbial growthOnly small substrates can enter
✅ Advantages
  • Cheap and simple method
  • Large quantities of enzymes can be immobilized by encapsulation
  • Enzyme is well protected inside the capsule
❌ Disadvantages
  • Pore size limitation — only small substrate molecules can cross the membrane
  • Not suitable for enzymes that act on large macromolecular substrates (e.g., proteases acting on intact proteins)
  • Membrane can break or become fouled over time

Section 06

Immobilization of Whole Cells

Instead of extracting and purifying individual enzymes, it is sometimes more practical or economical to immobilize the entire microbial cell that contains the desired enzyme(s). This is the concept of whole cell immobilization.

Why Immobilize Whole Cells?

📌 Rationale

Whole cell immobilization is an alternative to enzyme immobilization. It is the preferred approach when:

  1. Individual enzymes become inactive during immobilization — extracting the enzyme from the cell and then immobilizing it denatures it; the cell environment is more protective
  2. Isolation and purification of the enzyme is not cost-effective — enzyme purification can be expensive (especially multi-step chromatographic purification); avoiding extraction saves money and time
  3. Multi-enzyme reactions are needed — the cell naturally contains all the enzymes required for a metabolic pathway; immobilizing the whole cell gives access to all of them simultaneously

Important Features of Whole Cell Immobilization

  • It is a well-developed method for the utilization of enzymes from microbes
  • Enzymes within the whole cell remain active and stable for a long period of time — the natural cellular environment protects them
  • The methods of cell immobilization are essentially the same as described for enzyme immobilization
  • The adsorption method is the oldest method for cell immobilization — historically, woodchips were used as a carrier for vinegar production (immobilized Acetobacter on wood chips)
✅ Advantages of Whole Cell Immobilization
  • Multiple enzymes can be introduced in a single step — the whole metabolic pathway is available
  • Extraction and purification of enzymes are not required — saves time and cost
  • Enzymes are stable for long time within their natural environment
  • Native conformation of enzyme is best maintained — less denaturation risk
  • Cell organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts can be immobilized — allows use of complex multi-enzyme systems
❌ Disadvantages of Whole Cell Immobilization
  • Concentration of desired enzyme will be less — the enzyme of interest is diluted by all other cellular components
  • Production of unwanted enzymes and products — the cell makes many different enzymes, some of which may catalyze side reactions
  • Modification of end products by other enzymes — enzymes other than the target may further process the desired product into an unwanted compound

Methods of Whole Cell Immobilization

The methods are similar to enzyme immobilization, with one additional method — cell-to-cell cross-linking:

💡 Unique Method — Cell-to-Cell Cross-Linking

In whole cell immobilization, there is an additional method not used for enzymes: Cell-to-Cell Cross-Linking. Here, cells are directly cross-linked to each other (not to a support) using bifunctional reagents like glutaraldehyde. This creates a dense, insoluble cellular aggregate that can act as a biocatalytic unit.

📋 Section Summary

Whole cell immobilization preserves multiple enzymes within their natural environment, avoids costly purification, and maintains native enzyme conformation. Five methods apply: adsorption (oldest — woodchips), covalent bonding, cell-to-cell cross-linking (unique to cells), entrapment, and encapsulation. Main drawbacks: lower concentration of desired enzyme, risk of side reactions by other cellular enzymes.

Section 07

Industrial Case Study: High Fructose Syrup Production

This is one of the most important and commercially significant applications of immobilized enzymes. It demonstrates how thermostable enzymes and immobilized enzymes work together in a multi-step industrial process.

🏭 Process Overview

The goal is to convert cheap corn starch into a high fructose syrup (much sweeter than glucose syrup) for use as a sweetener in confectionery and drinks. Fructose is approximately 1.7× sweeter than glucose, making this conversion commercially very valuable.

Step-by-Step Process

1

Starch Paste Preparation

Raw corn starch is prepared as a starch paste — starch dissolved/suspended in hot water to form a viscous mixture.

2

Liquefaction with Thermostable α-Amylase 90°C / few hours

The starch paste is incubated with the thermostable enzyme alpha-amylase (α-amylase) at 90°C for a couple of hours. α-Amylase catalyzes the hydrolysis of α-1,4 glycosidic bonds in starch, breaking it down into shorter glucose chains called dextrins. The enzyme must be thermostable to withstand this high temperature.

3

Deactivation of α-Amylase 140°C

The temperature is raised to 140°C to denature (inactivate) the α-amylase. This is necessary to stop the α-amylase reaction before the next enzyme is added — otherwise it would compete or interfere with the next step.

4

Saccharification with Amyloglucosidase ~55°C

Temperature is lowered to approximately 55°C. The fungal enzyme amyloglucosidase (also called glucoamylase) is added. It catalyzes the hydrolysis of dextrins — both α-1,4 and α-1,6 glycosidic bonds — releasing individual glucose molecules. The result is a concentrated glucose syrup.

5

Isomerization with Immobilized Glucose Isomerase Key Immobilization Step

The glucose syrup is converted to fructose syrup using the enzyme glucose isomerase. This is the crucial step where immobilization technology is employed:

  • Glucose isomerase is immobilized in rigid granules
  • These granules are packed into a column (column reactor)
  • Glucose syrup is poured into the top of the column
  • As it flows through, it contacts the immobilized enzyme and is isomerized
  • Fructose syrup emerges from the bottom of the column, free from contamination with enzyme

Why is Enzyme Immobilization Critical in This Process?

Problem with Free EnzymeHow Immobilization Solves It
Enzyme contaminates the fructose syrup (must be removed — costly)Immobilized enzyme stays in column; product exits clean
Enzyme used only once then discarded (expensive)Column enzyme is reused continuously — far cheaper
Batch process — stop, clean, restartContinuous process — glucose syrup flows in, fructose flows out 24/7
Inconsistent product qualityConsistent isomerization conditions → consistent product
💡 Why Thermostable Enzymes for Steps 1–3?

Starch has a very high viscosity at room temperature. At 90°C, the starch paste liquefies, making it easier for the enzyme to access and hydrolyze. However, most enzymes denature at 90°C — this is why a thermostable α-amylase is essential. Thermostable enzymes are typically derived from thermophilic bacteria (e.g., Bacillus licheniformis) that naturally live at high temperatures. Their proteins have evolved to maintain their structure and function at extreme heat.

📋 Section Summary — High Fructose Syrup

5-step process: Starch paste → α-amylase at 90°C → dextrins → heat to 140°C (deactivate amylase) → cool to 55°C, add amyloglucosidase → glucose syrup → glucose isomerase (immobilized in column) → fructose syrup. Key advantage of immobilization: enzyme reuse, no product contamination, continuous production.

Section 09

Master Revision Sheet

A condensed, high-yield summary for rapid review before exams. Every key fact is captured here.

🔑 Definitions
  • Immobilization = imprisonment of enzyme/cell in support/matrix
  • Medium exchanges freely through the support
  • First use: amino acylases by Aspergillus oryzae, Japan
  • Two main advantages: functional efficiency + reproducibility
📊 9 Advantages of Immobilized Enzymes
  • Reuse; Continuous use; Less labour
  • Capital cost savings; Minimum reaction time
  • Less contamination; More stability
  • Improved process control
  • High enzyme-substrate ratio
🏭 8 Applications
  • Industrial (antibiotics, beverages, amino acids)
  • Biomedical (diagnosis, treatment, drug delivery)
  • Food (jams, jellies, syrups)
  • Research (HRP in blotting)
  • Biodiesel (lipase on vegetable oils)
  • Wastewater management
  • Textile (scouring, bio-polishing, desizing)
  • Detergent (lipase)
🧱 3 Matrix Categories
  • Natural: alginate, chitosan, collagen, carrageenan, gelatin, cellulose (cheapest), starch, pectin
  • Synthetic: ion exchange resins, DEAE cellulose, PVC, UV-PEG
  • Inorganic: zeolites, ceramics, diatomaceous earth (Celite), silica, glass, activated carbon, charcoal
🔬 Method 1: Adsorption
  • Oldest & simplest method
  • Nelson & Griffin: charcoal + invertase
  • Bonds: ionic, H-bond, Van der Waals
  • 4 sub-methods: static, dynamic batch, reactor loading, electrode position
  • Carrier: 500 Å – 1 mm; no pore diffusion limit
  • Adv: cheap, easy, gentle on protein
  • Disadv: desorption, low efficiency
⚗️ Method 2: Covalent Bonding
  • Most widely used method
  • Strong covalent bonds enzyme ↔ support
  • 9 functional groups: α-COOH, α-NH₂, ε-NH₂ (Lys/Arg), β/γ-COOH (Asp/Glu), phenol (Tyr), –SH (Cys), –OH (Ser/Thr), imidazole (His), indole (Trp)
  • OH & NH₂ form bonds most easily
  • 3 methods: diazoation, peptide bond, polyfunctional reagents (glutaraldehyde)
  • Disadv: active site modification risk → use substrate/competitive inhibitor to protect
🕸️ Method 3: Entrapment
  • Physically trapped inside matrix
  • No chemical modification of enzyme
  • Matrix: polyacrylamide, cellulose triacetate, agar, gelatin, carrageenan, alginate
  • 3 sub-methods: gels, fibers, microcapsules
  • Pore size adjustable by polymer concentration
  • Good for sensing; limited industrial use
  • Disadv: leakage (esp. small MW enzymes), pore diffusion limit, microbial contamination
🔗 Methods 4 & 5: Cross-linking & Encapsulation
  • Cross-Linking: enzyme-to-enzyme covalent bonds; no support; uses glutaraldehyde / diazonium salt; cheap; risk of denaturation
  • Encapsulation: enclosed in semi-permeable membrane capsule (nitrocellulose or nylon); only small substrates enter; large quantity can be immobilized; pore size limitation
🦠 Whole Cell Immobilization
  • Alternative to enzyme immobilization
  • Best when: enzyme inactivates on isolation OR isolation is too costly
  • Same methods as enzyme + cell-to-cell cross-linking
  • Oldest = adsorption (woodchips carrier)
  • Adv: multiple enzymes, no purification, long stability, native conformation
  • Disadv: low enzyme concentration, unwanted enzymes, side products
🍬 High Fructose Syrup — Key Facts
  • Corn starch → glucose → fructose
  • Step 1: α-amylase (thermostable) at 90°C → dextrins
  • Step 2: Heat to 140°C → deactivate α-amylase
  • Step 3: Amyloglucosidase at 55°C → glucose
  • Step 4: Glucose isomerase (immobilized in column) → fructose syrup
  • Fructose exits column free from enzyme

High-Yield Exam Points

⚠️ Must-Know Facts for Exams
  • Adsorption = OLDEST & SIMPLEST; Covalent bonding = MOST WIDELY USED
  • Only Cross-Linking uses NO support/matrix
  • Entrapment and Encapsulation involve NO chemical modification of the enzyme
  • Cellulose = cheapest natural polymer support
  • Diatomaceous earth trade name = Celite
  • CNBr-agarose and CNBr-Sepharose = classic covalent bonding supports in labs
  • Glutaraldehyde = bifunctional (2 aldehyde groups); used in both covalent bonding AND cross-linking
  • To prevent active site damage in covalent bonding → immobilize in presence of substrate or competitive inhibitor
  • High fructose syrup uses glucose isomerase immobilized in GRANULES in a COLUMN
  • Whole cell: extra method = cell-to-cell cross-linking
  • Agar & carrageenan have LARGE pore sizes (compared to polyacrylamide)
  • Encapsulation capsule material: nitrocellulose OR nylon
  • Hybridoma cells immobilized in alginate polylysine → monoclonal antibody production
  • Adsorption particle size: 500 Å to 1 mm diameter
⭐ The Fundamental Trade-off in Immobilization

All immobilization methods involve a trade-off between binding strength and enzyme activity preservation:

  • Adsorption: Weakest binding → enzyme activity best preserved → but enzyme easily detaches
  • Covalent bonding: Strongest binding → no leakage → but risk of active site modification → activity may decrease
  • Entrapment/Encapsulation: Middle ground → no enzyme modification → physical diffusion limitations remain the challenge
  • Cross-linking: Very strong → no carrier needed → but polyfunctional reagents may denature enzyme

The choice of method therefore depends on the enzyme type, the substrate size, the required stability, and the cost constraints of the application.
