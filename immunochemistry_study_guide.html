Section 01

Immunology

Before birth, the human foetus lives in a practically sterile environment within the uterus. After birth, we are exposed to a vast and diverse range of potentially harmful agents throughout our entire lifespan. The survival of the human race depends on the body's remarkable ability to recognise, fight, and eliminate these threats — a capability governed by the immune system.

1.1 What is Immunology?

Definition

Immunology is the scientific study of immunity — the physiological process by which the body protects itself from injurious agents. The vast majority of these agents are infectious organisms including bacteria, viruses, protozoa, and fungi.

The immune system is one of the most sophisticated biological systems ever evolved. It must be capable of detecting an enormous variety of threats while simultaneously tolerating the body's own tissues (the concept of "self"). When this system fails — either by under-responding or over-responding — disease results.

Threats Recognised by the Immune System

The immune system is designed to combat a wide spectrum of foreign invaders and abnormal host cells:

🦠

Bacteria

Single-celled prokaryotic microbes that can cause infections ranging from mild to life-threatening.

🔬

Viruses

Non-cellular pathogens that hijack host cell machinery to replicate and spread.

🦟

Protozoa

Single-celled eukaryotic parasites (e.g., Plasmodium causing malaria).

🍄

Fungi

Eukaryotic organisms; normally harmless but dangerous in immunocompromised individuals.

🪱

Parasitic Worms

Multicellular helminths that can cause chronic inflammatory responses.

☠️

Toxins

Poisonous substances produced by pathogens (e.g., bacterial toxins like tetanus toxin).

🎗️

Cancer Cells

Abnormal host cells with mutated surface markers; the immune system attempts to destroy them.

🏥

Iatrogenic Threats

Foreign materials introduced through medical procedures (e.g., transplanted organs, drug reactions).

1.2 Self vs. Non-Self Recognition

Perhaps the most critical capability of the immune system is its ability to distinguish between "self" (the body's own cells and proteins) and "non-self" (foreign agents). This recognition is accomplished by two main molecular tools:

🔵 Surface Immunoglobulin (sIg) on B-Lymphocytes

B-cells display antibody molecules (called surface immunoglobulins) on their plasma membranes. These act as antigen receptors for B-cells. When sIg binds a matching antigen, B-cell activation and antibody secretion is triggered.

🟣 T-Cell Receptors (TCR) on T-Lymphocytes

T-lymphocytes carry T-cell receptors — membrane-bound proteins structurally related to immunoglobulins. TCRs recognise antigen fragments (peptides) only when presented on the surface of host cells by MHC molecules.

Key Concept

Both sIg and TCR are antigen-recognition molecules. However, sIg can bind free, soluble antigen in its native form, while TCR only recognises antigen that has been processed and presented on the surface of an antigen-presenting cell (APC) in the context of MHC molecules. This fundamental difference shapes the distinct roles of B-cells and T-cells in immunity.

1.3 Main Components of the Immune System

The immune system is not a single organ — it is an integrated network of cells, proteins, and tissues distributed throughout the body. The primary components include:

1

Complement System

A cascade of ~30 serum proteins that, when activated, can directly lyse pathogens, opsonise them for phagocytosis, and recruit inflammatory cells. Activated by antibodies (classical pathway) or directly by pathogen surfaces (alternative pathway).

2

Cytokines

Small signalling proteins (e.g., interleukins, interferons, TNF) that coordinate the immune response. They act as messengers between immune cells, regulating activation, proliferation, and differentiation.

3

Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)

Glycoproteins produced by plasma cells (differentiated B-cells). They bind specifically to antigens and facilitate their neutralisation and elimination. The central topic of this lecture.

4

Phagocytes

Cells such as neutrophils and macrophages that engulf and destroy pathogens. Opsonisation by antibodies dramatically enhances phagocytic efficiency.

5

Lymphocytes

The adaptive immune system's primary cells — B-lymphocytes (humoral immunity, antibody production) and T-lymphocytes (cell-mediated immunity). Natural Killer (NK) cells are a third type providing innate cytotoxicity.

6

Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs)

Cells such as dendritic cells, macrophages, and B-cells that process antigens and display fragments on their surface via MHC class II molecules to activate helper T-cells (CD4⁺).

Section 1 Summary
  • Immunology studies how the body defends itself against bacteria, viruses, protozoa, fungi, parasites, toxins, cancer, and iatrogenic threats.
  • The foetus lives in a sterile uterine environment; lifelong immune challenges begin after birth.
  • Self vs non-self recognition is mediated by sIg (on B-cells) and TCR (on T-cells).
  • Main components: complement system, cytokines, antibodies, phagocytes, lymphocytes, APCs.
Section 02

Antibodies

2.1 Definition & Classification

Definition

Antibodies (also called immunoglobulins, Ig) are globulin glycoproteins produced by plasma cells in response to antigen stimulation. Each antibody reacts specifically with the antigen that stimulated its production. They are found predominantly in blood plasma and other body fluids.

Classification by Electrophoretic Migration

Blood serum proteins can be separated into fractions by electrophoresis. Three major globulin bands are identified:

Key Fact

Antibodies are gamma globulins. They constitute approximately 20% of total plasma protein. In clinical practice, a decrease in gamma globulins (hypogammaglobulinaemia) indicates immune deficiency, while an increase (hypergammaglobulinaemia) may indicate chronic infection or certain cancers (e.g., myeloma).

Deep Dive: Each Immunoglobulin Class

IgG — The Most Abundant
  • Comprises ~75% of serum immunoglobulins — the dominant antibody in secondary immune responses.
  • The only antibody that crosses the placenta (transplacental passage), providing passive immunity to the newborn for the first few months of life.
  • Activates complement (classical pathway) and facilitates opsonisation (marking pathogens for phagocytosis).
  • Has four subclasses: IgG1, IgG2, IgG3, IgG4, each with slightly different properties.
  • Molecular weight: ~150 kDa. Sedimentation coefficient: 7S.
IgA — The Secretory Antibody
  • Second most abundant in serum (~15%), but the most abundant antibody in secretions (saliva, tears, colostrum, breast milk, intestinal and respiratory secretions).
  • Exists as a monomer in serum; forms a dimer (linked by J chain and secretory component) in secretions.
  • Primary role: prevent mucosal invasion by pathogens — first line of defence at mucous membranes.
  • Does not fix complement (classical pathway); does not cross the placenta.
  • The dimeric form (11S) is found in secretions; monomeric form (7S) in serum.
IgM — The Primary Response Antibody
  • The first antibody produced in the primary immune response to a new antigen.
  • Normally exists as a pentamer (5 monomers linked by J chains via disulfide bonds) — giving it 10 potential antigen-binding sites.
  • Despite 10 antigen-binding sites, effective valency is often less due to steric hindrance.
  • Extremely efficient at complement activation — one IgM molecule can activate complement (vs. two IgG needed).
  • Also serves as the antigen receptor on B-cells (monomeric form on B-cell surface).
  • Largest immunoglobulin: MW ~900 kDa; sedimentation coefficient 19S.
  • Cannot cross the placenta due to large size. Presence of IgM in a newborn indicates in utero infection.
  • Opsonises indirectly — by activating complement to produce C3b (an opsonin).
IgD — The B-Cell Receptor
  • Present in very low concentrations in serum (~3 mg/dL; 0.2% of total Ig).
  • Found primarily on the surface of naïve B-lymphocytes alongside IgM — its role as antigen receptor is confirmed; B-cell surface role is marked with ? in Table 59-1, indicating some uncertainty historically.
  • Functions as part of the B-cell receptor (BCR) complex, signalling B-cell activation.
  • Does not fix complement, does not cross placenta, not found in secretions.
IgE — The Allergy & Antiparasitic Antibody
  • Present at vanishingly small concentrations in healthy individuals (~0.05 μg/mL).
  • Dramatically elevated in allergic diseases (asthma, hay fever, anaphylaxis) and parasitic worm (helminth) infections.
  • Mediates allergic (hypersensitivity Type I) responses: binds to Fc receptors on mast cells and basophils via its Fc region. When cross-linked by antigen, triggers degranulation → release of histamine, leukotrienes → allergy symptoms.
  • Does not fix complement. Does not cross placenta.

2.3 Functions of Antibodies

Antibodies perform four major protective functions, plus an additional catalytic role:

🛡️

Neutralisation

Antibodies (especially IgG and IgA) bind to toxins and viruses, blocking their ability to interact with host cell receptors. The pathogen/toxin is rendered harmless without being destroyed directly.

🎯

Opsonisation

IgG coats the surface of microbes. Phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages) carry Fc receptors that bind the IgG Fc region, dramatically enhancing engulfment. IgM opsonises indirectly via complement C3b.

⚙️

Complement Activation

IgG and IgM fix complement via the classical pathway. The complement cascade produces C3b (opsonin), C3a/C5a (anaphylatoxins), and the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC) that lyses pathogens.

🚫

Prevention of Mucosal Attachment

Secretory IgA (sIgA) in mucosal secretions blocks pathogen attachment to epithelial surfaces — a critical first-line barrier in the gut, lungs, and urogenital tract.

⚗️

Catalytic Activity (Abzymes)

Some antibodies possess enzymatic (catalytic) activity and are called "abzymes." This was an unexpected discovery showing antibodies can catalyse specific chemical reactions — of potential therapeutic use.

J Chain — What is it?

The J (joining) chain is a small polypeptide (MW ~15 kDa) that holds polymeric immunoglobulins together. It links IgM pentamers and IgA dimers via disulfide bonds. Without the J chain, these polymeric forms cannot assemble properly. The J chain is produced by the same plasma cell that secretes the antibody.

  • Five classes: IgG (75%), IgA (15%), IgM (9%), IgD (0.2%), IgE (0.004%).
  • Classified by heavy chain type: γ, α, μ, δ, ε respectively.
  • Functions: neutralisation, opsonisation, complement activation, mucosal protection, catalytic activity.
  • IgG → crosses placenta; IgA → secretions; IgM → primary response/pentamer; IgE → allergy.
  • IgG and IgM fix complement; only IgG passes placenta; only IgA found in secretions.
Section 03

Immunoglobulin Structure

The architecture of an antibody molecule is one of the most elegant in all of biochemistry — a Y-shaped glycoprotein perfectly engineered to bind an almost infinite variety of antigens with exquisite specificity, while simultaneously signalling the rest of the immune system for elimination.

3.1 Light and Heavy Chains

Definition — Basic Architecture

The simplest antibody molecule (a monomer) consists of four polypeptide chains:

  • Two identical Heavy (H) chains
  • Two identical Light (L) chains

These four chains are held together by disulfide (–S–S–) bonds and non-covalent forces, forming a characteristic Y shape.

⚡ Light (L) Chains

  • Molecular weight: ~25,000 Da (25 kDa)
  • Two types: kappa (κ) and lambda (λ)
  • A single antibody molecule has EITHER two κ or two λ chains — never mixed.
  • Composed of 1 variable domain (VL) + 1 constant domain (CL)
  • The constant region of the L chain has no known biologic function.

💪 Heavy (H) Chains

  • Molecular weight: 50,000–70,000 Da (50–70 kDa)
  • Five types determining the Ig class: γ, α, μ, δ, ε
  • An antibody always has two identical H chains.
  • Composed of 1 variable domain (VH) + 3 or 4 constant domains
  • IgG and IgA: 3 CH domains (CH1, CH2, CH3)
  • IgM and IgE: 4 CH domains (extra domain replaces the hinge)
Critical Rule

An individual antibody molecule always consists of identical H chains and identical L chains. You will never have two different H chain types or two different L chain types in the same antibody molecule. This ensures structural symmetry and gives the antibody two identical antigen-binding sites (bivalency).

IgM and IgE have four constant heavy chain domains (CH1 through CH4). They lack a hinge region — the extra CH2 domain provides flexibility instead. This accounts for their higher molecular weight compared to other monomeric antibodies.

Common Misconception

Students often assume the constant region of the light chain has a function — it does not. All biologic effector functions are mediated exclusively by the constant region of the heavy chain. The light chain's constant region appears to serve primarily a structural role.

3.4 Hypervariable Regions and the Antigen-Binding Site

Hypervariable Regions (Complementarity-Determining Regions — CDRs)

Within the variable regions of both L and H chains, there are three extremely variable amino acid sequences called hypervariable regions (also called CDRs — Complementarity-Determining Regions). These are located at the amino-terminal end of the variable domains.

Key Facts About CDRs

  • Each variable domain (VL and VH) contains 3 CDR loops → total of 6 CDRs per antigen-binding site.
  • The CDRs from the VL and VH domains come together in 3D space to form the antigen-binding site (also called the paratope).
  • Only 5 to 10 amino acids in each hypervariable region directly contact the antigen — these are the "hot spots" of binding.
  • The remarkable specificity of antibodies — the ability to distinguish between nearly identical antigens — is entirely due to the sequence diversity of these hypervariable regions.
  • The human immune system can generate an estimated 10¹⁰ to 10¹¹ distinct antibody specificities through the combinatorial diversity of V(D)J gene recombination, junctional diversity, and somatic hypermutation.

3.5 Antigen–Antibody Binding: The Non-Covalent Forces

The interaction between antibody and antigen is highly specific but reversible — it relies entirely on non-covalent bonds, not covalent bonds. Multiple types of weak interactions work synergistically to achieve high-affinity binding:

Electrostatic (Ionic) Forces

Attraction between oppositely charged amino acid residues (e.g., –NH₃⁺ and –COO⁻). Highly distance-dependent.

Van der Waals Forces

Weak, transient dipole–dipole attractions between all atoms in close proximity. Weak individually; powerful in sum across a large contact area.

Hydrogen Bonds

Partial sharing of hydrogen atoms between electronegative atoms (O, N). Particularly important for polar antigens.

Hydrophobic Bonds

Non-polar residues cluster together to exclude water — driven by entropy. Critical when antigen epitopes are hydrophobic (e.g., lipid-based antigens).

Why Non-Covalent?

The use of reversible non-covalent bonds is functionally essential. Covalent bonds would permanently trap the antibody on the antigen surface, preventing recycling. Non-covalent binding allows the antibody–antigen complex to form, perform its function (e.g., complement activation, phagocytosis signalling), and then potentially dissociate. The affinity of an antibody reflects the sum strength of all these non-covalent interactions at one antigen-binding site.

3.6 Immunoglobulin Fragments: Fab, Fc, and the Hinge Region

The Y-shaped antibody molecule can be conceptually and experimentally divided into functional regions:

FragmentFull NameCompositionFunction
Fab Fragment, Antigen Binding One entire L chain + VH and CH1 of H chain Binds antigen; monovalent (one antigen-binding site per Fab)
Fc Fragment, Crystallisable CH2 + CH3 domains of both H chains Complement activation, Fc receptor binding, placental transfer, half-life regulation
Hinge region — Proline-rich segment between CH1 and CH2 of H chain Provides flexibility between two Fab arms; contains interchain disulfide bonds; target of papain and pepsin cleavage
Why is Fc called "Crystallisable"?

The Fc fragment was named because when antibodies are digested with papain and stored in cold temperatures, the Fc fragments spontaneously crystallise. This happens because all Fc fragments from the same immunoglobulin class are structurally identical (no sequence variability like in Fab) — making them highly regular and prone to crystal formation. This property was what originally allowed researchers to study the Fc fragment in isolation.

Section 3 Summary
  • Antibody = 2 identical H chains + 2 identical L chains linked by disulfide bonds → Y-shape.
  • L chain: MW ~25 kDa; 1 VL + 1 CL domain. H chain: MW ~50–70 kDa; 1 VH + 3 (or 4) CH domains.
  • Each domain ≈ 110 amino acids, stabilised by intrachain disulfide bond.
  • Variable regions → antigen binding. Constant regions of H chain → effector functions.
  • CH2 = complement binding; CH3 = Fc receptor / placental transfer.
  • Hypervariable regions (CDRs) within VL and VH form the antigen-binding site; only 5–10 aa per CDR directly contact antigen.
  • Antigen–antibody binding: electrostatic + van der Waals + hydrogen + hydrophobic bonds — all non-covalent, reversible.
  • Fab = antigen binding; Fc = effector functions; Hinge = flexibility + disulfide bonds.
Section 04

Enzymatic Digestion of Antibodies

Much of what we know about antibody structure was elucidated through deliberate enzymatic and chemical fragmentation. By cutting the antibody at specific sites and analysing the resulting pieces, researchers determined which regions mediate antigen binding and which mediate effector functions. Three key experiments define this understanding.

4.1 Digestion with Papain

Papain — A Cysteine Protease

Papain is a cysteine endopeptidase (protease) derived from papaya. It cleaves peptide bonds on the amino-terminal side of the hinge region — specifically, it cuts above the disulfide bonds that link the two heavy chains in the hinge.

Products of Papain Digestion

Papain digestion of one IgG molecule yields three fragments:

2× Fab Fragments
  • Fab = Fragment, Antigen Binding
  • Each consists of one entire L chain + the VH and CH1 portion of one H chain
  • The two Fab fragments are identical
  • Each Fab is monovalent — it has only ONE antigen-binding site
  • Cannot cross-link antigens (important distinction from intact IgG)
  • MW ≈ 50 kDa each
1× Fc Fragment
  • Fc = Fragment, Crystallisable
  • Consists of CH2 and CH3 domains of both H chains, held together by disulfide bonds
  • Does NOT bind antigen
  • Contains all the effector function sites: complement activation (CH2), Fc receptor binding, placental transfer (CH3)
  • Crystallises in cold storage → hence the name "Fc"
Cleavage Site — Papain

Papain cleaves above (amino-terminal to) the interchain disulfide bonds in the hinge region. This is the critical distinction from pepsin. Result: the two H chains are separated at the Fc level, giving two individual Fab units plus one intact Fc dimer.

4.2 Digestion with Pepsin

Pepsin — An Aspartyl Protease

Pepsin is an aspartyl protease (found in stomach juice). It cleaves on the carboxy-terminal side of the hinge disulfide bonds — i.e., it cuts below the interchain disulfide bonds.

Products of Pepsin Digestion

F(ab')₂ Fragment
  • Both Fab regions remain covalently linked via the hinge disulfide bonds (not cleaved by pepsin)
  • Hence: F(ab')2 = two Fab' units still joined together
  • The prime (′) notation indicates the Fab retains part of the hinge region
  • Bivalent — has TWO antigen-binding sites → can cross-link antigens, form lattices/precipitin reactions
  • Used therapeutically (e.g., antivenom, digoxin antidote DigiFab) because it retains full antigen-binding but lacks Fc-mediated inflammatory side effects
Fc — Fully Degraded
  • The Fc portion is entirely digested by pepsin into small, non-recoverable peptide fragments
  • No Fc fragment is recovered from pepsin digestion
  • This is the key difference from papain: pepsin destroys the Fc
Clinical Relevance of F(ab')₂

F(ab')2 fragments are clinically valuable precisely because they lack the Fc region. When antibody fragments are used therapeutically (e.g., antivenoms, anti-rejection treatments), the Fc region can cause unwanted complement activation, immune cell recruitment, and inflammatory damage. F(ab')2 fragments retain full antigen-binding capability while eliminating these side effects.

4.3 Mercaptoethanol Reduction

Mercaptoethanol (β-Mercaptoethanol / BME)

2-Mercaptoethanol (BME) is a reducing agent that cleaves all disulfide bonds (–S–S–) by reducing them to free sulfhydryl groups (–SH). It does not cleave peptide bonds. When applied to an intact antibody, it disrupts both interchain and intrachain disulfide bonds.

Products of Mercaptoethanol Reduction

Since all disulfide bonds are broken, the four polypeptide chains held together by these bonds fall apart:

Two Identical H Chains
  • Each heavy chain is released as a separate polypeptide
  • Now carry free –SH groups where disulfides were
  • MW ≈ 50–70 kDa each
  • Can be separated and characterised individually
Two Identical L Chains
  • Each light chain is released as a separate polypeptide
  • Carry free –SH groups at previously disulfide-bonded cysteines
  • MW ≈ 25 kDa each
  • Can be separated from H chains by gel filtration or SDS-PAGE
Important Note

Mercaptoethanol reduction alone simply separates the chains — because intrachain disulfide bonds also stabilise each domain's 3D fold, reduction can also unfold the domains. In practice, a denaturing agent (like urea) is often added alongside BME to fully denature and separate the chains for analysis (e.g., SDS-PAGE to determine MW of each chain).

Section 4 Summary
  • Papain cleaves above hinge disulfide bonds → 2 Fab (monovalent, antigen-binding) + 1 Fc (crystallisable, effector functions).
  • Pepsin cleaves below hinge disulfide bonds → 1 F(ab')₂ (bivalent, antigen-binding) + Fc degraded entirely.
  • Mercaptoethanol reduces all disulfide bonds → 4 separate chains: 2 identical H chains + 2 identical L chains.
  • Fab = antigen binding but monovalent. F(ab')₂ = bivalent, no Fc, clinically useful.
  • Fc gets its name from crystallising in cold storage.
